# Competitive Brief: Mid-Market Field Service Evaluation

## Deal Context

The prospect is a field service company (construction, plumbing, electrical, or HVAC) with **10-100 employees and 5-15 trucks** currently evaluating inventory tracking tools. They've outgrown spreadsheets or manual tracking after a trigger event — a costly equipment loss, growth past 5 trucks, repeated job delays, or a painful annual audit. They're now comparing Sortly against a shortlist that likely includes InFlow Inventory, an asset tracking tool like Asset Panda or GoCodes, and the option of sticking with their current approach. The buyer is the owner or ops manager, and they're evaluating based on three criteria in this order: (1) does it work on a phone in the field, (2) will my least tech-savvy crew member use it, (3) how fast do I see value.

## Where We Win

- **Buyer is replacing spreadsheets, paper, or memory:** This is 80%+ of the segment. Sortly wins because mobile photo-based tracking is dramatically faster and more intuitive than updating a spreadsheet from a job site. The crew scans a barcode in 10 seconds from their truck — that's faster than calling the boss to ask where the pipe threader is. Spreadsheets fail because nobody updates them from the field. Sortly embeds the update into the workflow at the moment of use.

- **Primary pain is field visibility across trucks and job sites:** When the buyer's core problem is "I get three calls before 9 AM asking where things are," Sortly's truck-level visibility is purpose-built for this. Every tool gets a photo, a location (which truck, which site, which shelf), and a QR label. The owner checks Sortly instead of texting three crew leads and waiting.

- **Crew adoption is the make-or-break concern:** Every field service owner has tried some version of "let's be more organized" and watched it fail. Sortly's entire UX was designed around the 10-second scan-and-go interaction — point phone at barcode, tap, move on. That's the bar: is it faster than calling the office? For most crews, it is. No competitor in this space matches this combination of simplicity and mobile-native design.

- **Buyer values speed-to-value over feature depth:** Sortly delivers an "aha" moment in minutes — add a few items with photos, scan a barcode, see the inventory on your phone. No week-long setup project. No SKU mapping or warehouse configuration required. The free trial is often the entire evaluation.

## Where They're Perceived as Stronger — and the Reframe

### InFlow Inventory

- **"InFlow has purchase orders and supplier management":** Honest context — InFlow does have deeper purchasing workflow capabilities, including PO management and multi-warehouse SKU tracking. **The reframe:** Ask the buyer what their actual problem is. If the answer is "I need to know what's on each truck and stop losing tools," purchase order management doesn't solve that. InFlow was built for warehouse and retail inventory — stationary products on shelves with SKU numbers. Field service inventory is distributed across moving vehicles and rotating crews. InFlow's mobile experience is secondary to its desktop experience, and setup requires mapping SKUs and warehouse locations before you see any value. For a company that just needs to answer "which truck has the pipe threader?" — InFlow is solving the wrong problem in the wrong way.

- **"InFlow looks more 'serious' or 'enterprise'":** Some buyers equate complexity with capability. **The reframe:** The question isn't whether the tool looks serious — it's whether your crew will actually use it. A system that's too complex for field workers to adopt gives you no data, which gives you no visibility. You end up paying for software nobody uses. Sortly is simple because field service demands simplicity — your guys have 10 seconds between jobs, not 10 minutes at a desk.

### Asset Panda / GoCodes

- **"Asset Panda has depreciation tracking and IT asset lifecycle features":** True — Asset Panda and GoCodes are built for larger organizations managing IT assets, fixed-asset depreciation, and asset lifecycle workflows. **The reframe:** Those capabilities matter if you're tracking laptops and software licenses across an enterprise IT department. They don't matter if you're tracking pipe threaders and generators across 8 trucks. Asset Panda's price point and complexity are designed for a different buyer — companies with dedicated IT asset managers, not field service owners who need their plumber to scan a barcode from a truck cab. For this buyer's use case, you'd be paying more for features you'll never touch.

- **"GoCodes has GPS tracking on assets":** Some buyers are drawn to GPS because they imagine knowing exactly where every tool is at all times. **The reframe:** GPS tracking solves a different problem — high-value assets that move independently (trailers, heavy equipment). For tools and parts that move with crews on trucks, you don't need GPS on every item. You need to know which truck it's on and who checked it out. That's a workflow problem, not a hardware problem — and adding GPS tags to every tool in your fleet gets expensive fast without solving the "who had it last" question.

### Spreadsheets / Paper / "We Just Know"

- **"Our current system works fine — we just know where things are":** This is the hardest competitor because it feels free. **The reframe:** It works until it doesn't. The system is actually the owner's brain — and it breaks down the moment you add crews, trucks, or job sites faster than one person can track. The real question: how many hours a week do you spend answering "where is the [tool]?" calls? And what did the last annual audit reveal in write-offs? Most owners in this segment discover they're losing **$15K-$50K a year** in duplicate purchases, untracked tool loss, and idle crews — but it never shows up on a single line item. It's a $200 fitting order here, a $400 rental there, a half-day of four workers standing around. The cost is invisible until someone makes it visible.
