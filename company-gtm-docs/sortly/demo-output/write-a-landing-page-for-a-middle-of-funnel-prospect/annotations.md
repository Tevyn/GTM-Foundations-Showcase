# Annotations: Write a landing page for a middle of funnel prospect

---

**Section:** Who the landing page is written for

> [!v1-passage]
> Sortly — Simple, Visual Inventory Management for Businesses of All Sizes. The intuitive, mobile-first platform that helps your organization track, organize, and manage inventory and assets with ease.

> [!v2-passage]
> Stop Being Your Company's Human Inventory System. Every tool, every truck, every job site — visible from your phone in seconds. Sortly gives field service crews a 10-second scan-and-go system they actually use, so you stop fielding "where's the [thing]?" calls and start running your business.

**Challenge:** Top of Funnel Growth

**V1:** The original ICP covers seventeen industries and every company size, so the landing page opens with a headline that could describe any inventory tool for any buyer.

**V2:** The focused ICP targets one buyer type, so the page opens in that buyer's world — their trucks, their crews, their Monday morning — without ever labeling the segment.

**Difference:** One opens with a generic product description, the other opens with the buyer's daily reality.

**Impact:** Content written for everyone converts no one. Focus is what turns budget into pipeline.

**Evidence:** Companies with a clearly defined ICP see up to 68% higher ROI on their campaigns.

---

**Section:** How the landing page describes the problem

> [!v1-passage]
> Sortly lets you add photos to every item in your inventory, making it easy to identify what you're looking for at a glance. Our platform supports custom fields, folders, and tags so you can create an organized, visual catalog of everything your business owns.

> [!v2-passage]
> You started this company to build things — not to spend your mornings tracking down a pipe threader that could be on any of eight trucks. But somewhere between your third truck and your eighth, you became the person everyone calls when something goes missing. Three calls before 9 AM. Texts to crew leads who don't respond for an hour. Sometimes you drive between job sites just to put eyes on a piece of equipment.

**Challenge:** Engagement & Conversion

**V1:** The original problem-solution work stays at headline level, so the page skips the problem entirely and leads with a feature description any inventory vendor could claim.

**V2:** The focused problem-solution work maps how the pain actually plays out day to day, so the page describes a situation the reader recognizes as their own before mentioning the product.

**Difference:** One lists capabilities without naming the problem, the other describes the specific mechanism that burns the buyer's time.

**Impact:** Buyers scroll past a feature list that names no problem they recognize. They engage when a page describes their morning back to them.

**Evidence:** Problem-centered messaging is associated with 47% higher buyer engagement.

**Section:** The proof each version offers

> [!v1-passage]
> "Great product, super easy to use and set up. Highly recommend for anyone looking to get organized." — Operations Manager. Trusted by 15,000+ customers across 28+ industries, including teams at Schneider IT, Sephora, Chewy, and Terminix.

**V1:** The original problem-solution work has no specific proof, so the page defaults to a generic praise quote and a logo list that signals "we sell to everyone."

**V2:** The focused problem-solution work includes before-and-after outcomes, so the proof quote describes a specific transformation the reader can picture happening in their own business.

**Difference:** One offers generic praise and a logo list, the other tells a before-and-after story from the buyer's world.

**Impact:** Generic praise quotes don't build trust. Before-and-after stories are what convert and retain.

**Evidence:** Specific, buyer-relevant messaging is tied to 2.3x higher lead-to-customer conversion.

# Reveal

## Top of Funnel Growth

**ICP:** Most companies define their ICP as an industry list and a company size range — so every team defines "our customer" differently. When ICP work identifies the specific buyer, their triggers, and their priorities, every asset can speak directly to that person — and that specificity is what focuses budget and drives pipeline.

**Messaging:** Most companies have a different value story on their website, in their sales deck, and in their outreach — so buyers hear three different products. When messaging is organized around a single idea, every touchpoint reinforces the same promise — and that consistency is what compounds into recognition and pipeline.

## Engagement & Conversion

**Problem-solution:** Most companies describe their problem at headline level — "inventory management challenges" or "tracking issues" — so their content sounds like every other vendor. When problem-solution work maps the specific mechanism that burns time and money, content can describe the buyer's real situation — and that specificity is what earns engagement and drives conversion.

**Positioning:** Most companies lead with what their product does instead of why the typical approach fails — so every vendor in the category sounds interchangeable. When positioning explains why the status quo breaks down, the page earns consideration by reframing the problem — and that differentiation is what converts browsers into buyers.

## Retention

**Problem-solution:** Most companies use generic customer quotes that could apply to any product — so their proof doesn't build trust with a specific buyer. When problem-solution work includes before-and-after outcomes tied to a specific problem, customer proof tells a story the prospect can see themselves in — and that relevance is what builds the trust that retains customers.

**Messaging:** Most companies shift their value story between acquisition, sales, and customer success — so expectations set in marketing don't match post-sale experience. When messaging is anchored to a single narrative, the promise stays consistent from first impression through renewal — and that consistency is what makes customers stay and refer.
