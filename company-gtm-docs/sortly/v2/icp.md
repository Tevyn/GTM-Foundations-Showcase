# Sortly — Ideal Customer Profile

## Overview
This ICP targets field service companies (construction, plumbing, electrical, HVAC) with 10-100 employees who manage assets and consumables across trucks, warehouses, and job sites. The core thesis: these businesses outgrow spreadsheets fast because their inventory is physically distributed across moving vehicles and rotating crews — creating a visibility gap that costs real money in lost tools, duplicate purchases, and job delays. Sortly's mobile-first, photo-based approach is the strongest fit for this segment because field crews need something they can use from a truck cab, not a desktop.

## Primary Segment

### Firmographics
**Industry:** Field services — specifically construction (general contractors, specialty trades), plumbing, electrical, and HVAC companies. These share a common operational model: mobile crews, truck-based inventory, multi-site work, and consumable-heavy workflows. We're clustering these because the inventory pain is structurally identical across them — the asset is on a truck, at a job site, or in a warehouse, and nobody knows which.
**Company Size:** 10-100 employees (typically 3-15 trucks/service vehicles in the fleet)
**Revenue:** $1M-$20M annually — large enough to have meaningful inventory investment ($50K-$1M+ in tools, equipment, and parts) but not large enough to justify or staff a dedicated inventory management function
**Geography:** US and Canada, primarily. Field service companies in this range are overwhelmingly domestic, and Sortly's English-first mobile experience and US-centric integrations (QuickBooks) align here.

### Technographics
**QuickBooks (Online or Desktop):** The dominant accounting tool for SMB field services. Its presence signals financial process maturity and an integration trigger — companies using QuickBooks want inventory data to flow into their books without double entry. Companies still doing paper invoicing are typically too early for Sortly.

**Spreadsheets or paper-based inventory tracking:** This is the primary "tool" being replaced. Field service companies tracking tools and parts in Excel, Google Sheets, clipboards, or memory represent the sweet spot — they have enough inventory to feel pain but haven't invested in a system yet. Companies already running a full ERP or heavy inventory system (NetSuite, Fishbowl) are likely over-scaled for Sortly's tier.

**Dispatching or job management software (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Jobber):** The presence of a dispatch tool signals operational maturity and confirms the multi-crew, multi-site workflow that creates inventory visibility pain. Companies using these tools have already digitized scheduling — inventory tracking is the next gap they'll feel.

**Smartphones issued to or expected from field crews:** Sortly's value depends on field adoption. Companies where crews already use phones for job photos, time tracking, or dispatch are ready for mobile inventory. Companies where field workers don't carry or use smartphones will struggle with adoption.

### Buying Triggers
**Trigger 1 — A costly tool or equipment loss event.** A $2,000 concrete saw goes missing from a truck. A $5,000 generator can't be located across three job sites. The owner or ops manager spends a full day calling crews and driving between sites. This single event — where the cost of the lost item plus the time spent searching exceeds their comfort threshold — creates immediate urgency. Below threshold: occasional misplacement of small hand tools ($50-200) that crews replace without escalation. That's annoying but not urgent enough to change systems.

**Trigger 2 — Growth from 1-3 trucks to 5+ trucks.** When a company adds crews, the owner can no longer personally track what's on each truck. The "I know where everything is" system breaks down. This is structural — it's not about a bad day, it's about the business outgrowing the owner's mental inventory. Below threshold: companies with 1-2 trucks where the owner rides along and visually audits daily. They have the pain but can manage it manually.

**Trigger 3 — Repeated project delays from missing parts or materials.** A plumber arrives at a job site without the right fittings. A GC's crew discovers the drill press they expected on the truck was left at the last site. When these delays happen 2-3 times per month and each one costs a half-day of labor plus customer frustration, the operations manager starts searching for a solution. Below threshold: one-off incidents that get chalked up to bad luck rather than a systems problem.

**Trigger 4 — End-of-year inventory audit reveals major discrepancies.** The annual "where is everything?" exercise takes a full week of downtime, and the write-off figure shocks the owner. $15K-$50K in unaccounted tools and equipment triggers a "never again" reaction. Below threshold: small discrepancies ($1K-$3K) that are absorbed as a cost of doing business.

### Deal Economics
**ACV Range:** $600-$6,000/year (Advanced plan at ~$50/month for small teams up to Enterprise for larger organizations with many users)
**Deal Cycle:** 1-14 days. These buyers move fast. The free trial is often the entire evaluation. They don't run RFPs — they Google, try, and buy. Larger companies (50-100) may take 2-4 weeks with a brief internal discussion.
**Buying Process Notes:** Self-serve trial in 9/10 deals. The owner or ops manager signs up, adds a few items with photos, and decides within a week. Enterprise deals (100 employees) may involve a brief call with sales. No procurement team, no security review, no legal redline at this tier.

## Pain Points (Specific to This Segment)

- **"Where's the [tool/equipment]?" syndrome:** Field crews can't locate high-value assets spread across trucks, job sites, and the warehouse. The owner or ops manager becomes the human inventory system — fielding calls, texting crews, sometimes driving between sites to physically locate equipment. → This isn't just an annoyance. Every search-and-locate cycle burns 30-90 minutes of the owner's time (their most expensive resource), and when the asset isn't found, it triggers a rental or emergency purchase at premium cost. At scale (5+ trucks, dozens of high-value assets), this becomes a daily drain on profitability.

- **Duplicate purchasing from invisible inventory:** Without visibility into what's on each truck and in the warehouse, crews buy parts and consumables they already have. The plumber orders $300 in copper fittings that are sitting in another truck. The electrician buys a new multimeter because the shared one can't be located. → Duplicate purchasing is invisible on a per-incident basis ($50-$500) but compounds to thousands per year. The economic buyer sees inflated material costs on the P&L but can't diagnose the root cause because there's no single source of truth for what's owned, where it is, and whether it's available.

- **Job site delays from missing or unavailable equipment:** A crew arrives to find they don't have the right tool, the generator is out of fuel, or the parts they expected were used on yesterday's job. The job stalls. → Each delay cascades: the crew sits idle (labor cost), the customer sees a crew standing around (reputation cost), and the project timeline slips (revenue delay). For a company billing $75-$150/hour per crew, a half-day delay is $300-$600 in unbilled labor plus the downstream scheduling disruption.

- **No accountability for check-out/check-in:** Tools and equipment move between crews with no record. When something breaks or goes missing, there's no audit trail — just finger-pointing. → Without accountability, loss rates stay high because there's no behavioral incentive to return items in good condition. The operations manager can't identify patterns (which crew loses the most tools, which items disappear most often) and therefore can't intervene. The business absorbs preventable loss as a fixed cost.

- **Inventory audits are a dreaded, manual ordeal:** Annual or semi-annual audits require pulling crews off jobs for 1-3 days to physically count and reconcile. The process is error-prone because it depends on memory and manual lists. → Audit downtime costs labor revenue (crews not billing), reveals write-offs that hit the P&L at once, and the results are immediately stale because inventory starts moving again the next day. Companies delay audits because of the cost, which makes the discrepancy worse over time.

## Competitive Landscape
**Spreadsheets / paper / memory (primary alternative):** The status quo for 80%+ of this segment. Buyers describe it as "we just know where things are" or "we keep a list." It works until it doesn't — the trigger events above are the moments when it breaks. Sortly wins against spreadsheets because mobile photo-based tracking is dramatically faster and more intuitive than updating a spreadsheet from a job site. Sortly loses when the company is too small (1-2 trucks) or the owner truly does have everything in their head.

**InFlow Inventory:** A direct competitor that targets SMBs but leans heavier toward warehouse/retail inventory management. InFlow is more complex, more desktop-oriented, and better suited for companies managing SKU-heavy product inventory rather than field assets. Sortly wins for field service because of mobile-first simplicity and visual (photo) tracking. Sortly loses when the buyer's primary need is purchase order management or multi-warehouse SKU tracking.

**Asset Panda / GoCodes / other asset tracking tools:** Purpose-built asset tracking tools that overlap with Sortly's feature set. These tend to target larger companies, have higher price points, and emphasize IT asset management or fixed-asset depreciation tracking. Sortly wins on ease of use and price for the SMB field service buyer. Sortly could lose if the buyer's primary need is IT asset lifecycle management or depreciation reporting.

**Doing nothing / accepting the loss:** For many in this segment, the current approach is to absorb tool loss and duplicate purchases as a cost of doing business. This is the hardest "competitor" to beat — it requires making the cost visible before the buyer will act. The trigger events above are what crack this inertia.

| "My guys won't use anything complicated" | "Easy-to-use for businesses of all sizes" | Buyers are worried about field crew adoption, not company size. The objection is about their least-tech-savvy crew member. |
| "I spent half my Saturday doing inventory" | "Inventory management from any device" | Buyers feel the time cost personally — often as lost personal time. "Any device" is a feature; "get your Saturday back" is a message. |
| "We keep buying stuff we already have" | "Real-time visibility across all locations" | Buyers describe the symptom (duplicate buying). Sortly describes the capability (visibility). Bridge the gap by naming the symptom first. |

## Targeting Criteria (Marketing)

**Firmographic filters:**
- Industry: SIC/NAICS codes for plumbing, electrical, HVAC, general contracting, specialty trade contractors
- Company size: 10-100 employees
- Revenue: $1M-$20M
- Geography: US and Canada
- Signals: fleet vehicles visible in company profiles, multiple job site locations mentioned, field service or trade-related keywords in company description

**Behavioral/intent signals:**
- Searching for "tool tracking," "equipment tracking for construction," "inventory app for field service," "track tools on trucks"
- Active on trade forums, contractor communities, or trade association sites
- QuickBooks Online user (identifiable through tech-stack data providers)
- Recently posted job listings for additional field technicians or crew leads (growth signal)
- Google My Business showing 2+ service areas (multi-site signal)

## Qualification Framework (Sales)

- **"Tell me about how you keep track of your tools and equipment across your trucks and job sites today."** Listen for: manual processes (spreadsheets, memory, paper), frustration with the current approach, recent incidents of loss or misplacement. If they say "we have a pretty good system" without pain, they may not be acute enough.

- **"How many trucks or crews are you running right now, and has that number changed recently?"** Listen for: 5+ trucks/crews (confirms scale where manual tracking breaks), recent growth (trigger signal). Below 3-4 trucks, the pain may not be acute enough to drive purchase.

- **"What happened the last time a piece of equipment went missing or a crew showed up without the right tools?"** Listen for: specific stories with dollar costs, time costs, customer impact. The more vivid and recent the story, the higher the urgency. Vague answers suggest the pain is mild.

- **"If your crew leads had to scan or check out tools from a phone, how would that go over?"** Listen for: crew smartphone usage, tech comfort level, existing mobile tool adoption (dispatch apps, time tracking). If crews aren't using phones for work today, adoption will be the primary obstacle.

### Qualification Signals

- **High severity:** "We've written off $10K+ in tools this year." "I personally spend 5+ hours a week tracking down equipment." "We had a job delay last week because a crew didn't have the right tools." Specific dollar figures and time costs signal acute pain.
- **High urgency:** Recently added 2+ trucks or crews. Just completed a painful inventory audit. Lost a high-value asset ($2,000+) in the last month. New operations manager hired to "get things under control." Insurance claim filed for lost equipment.
- **High frequency:** "This happens every week." "We buy duplicates every month." Multiple crew leads complaining about the same issue. The problem is structural and persistent, not a one-time event.

## Negative ICP (Not a Fit)

| Profile | Why Not | Disqualification Signal |
|---------|---------|------------------------|
| Solo operators / 1-2 person crews | Problem isn't acute enough — owner can see and touch everything. ACV too low to justify any acquisition cost. Churn risk: they'll use the free tier and never convert. | "It's just me and one helper." No fleet vehicles. Revenue under $500K. |
| Large enterprises (500+ employees) | Sortly's feature set isn't built for enterprise complexity — no ERP integration, no asset depreciation, no multi-entity management. These companies need SAP, Oracle, or a purpose-built EAM system. | Procurement process, RFP requirement, IT security review, mentions of "asset lifecycle management" or "ERP integration." |
| Retail / e-commerce businesses | Different problem entirely — SKU management, sales channels, POS integration. Sortly can do some of this, but InFlow, Cin7, or Shopify inventory are better fits. Competing here means competing on features where Sortly is weaker. | Primary need is "product inventory" or "sales order management." Mentions Shopify, Amazon, or multichannel selling. |
| Companies already on a full ERP | They've already solved the inventory problem (or at least believe they have). Sortly would be redundant or viewed as a downgrade. Even if their ERP's inventory module is weak, the switching cost and political dynamics make this a bad deal. | "We use NetSuite / SAP / Fishbowl / Oracle." References to ERP modules for inventory. |
| IT-focused companies tracking digital assets | Sortly can technically track IT assets, but purpose-built ITAM tools (Snipe-IT, Asset Panda) are better fits. The buyer's language and evaluation criteria won't align with Sortly's field-service-optimized UX. | "We need to track laptops, licenses, and hardware refresh cycles." Mentions ITAM, CMDB, or asset depreciation. |

**How to handle near-misses:** A 120-person construction company or a field service firm already using a basic spreadsheet system may look like a fit on firmographics but fall outside the sweet spot. For over-sized prospects: explore whether a division or region could pilot. For spreadsheet-heavy prospects who are "happy enough": plant the seed with a specific cost-of-loss calculation and follow up when a trigger event occurs. Don't push — the trigger will do the selling.
