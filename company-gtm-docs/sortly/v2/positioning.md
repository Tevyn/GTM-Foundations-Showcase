# Sortly — Positioning

## Overview
Sortly is positioned as the inventory tracking system built for field service teams who manage tools, equipment, and materials across trucks and job sites — not warehouses, not retail shelves, not data centers. The positioning thesis: field service companies have been underserved by inventory tools because the category was built for stationary inventory, and the real problem for this buyer isn't "inventory management" — it's "knowing what's on every truck and every site, right now, from my phone." Sortly owns this position because its mobile-first, photo-based, scan-to-track design was built for the exact workflow these buyers live in — grab a tool, scan it, go. No competitor in the field service adjacency matches this combination of simplicity, mobility, and visual tracking at this price point.

## Product Vision
Field service companies have a deceptively expensive problem: their most valuable assets are always moving — on trucks, at job sites, in transit between crews — and nobody knows the full picture except the owner. That worked when the company had two trucks. At eight trucks, fifteen trucks, the owner becomes the bottleneck, and the business bleeds money through lost tools, duplicate purchases, and crews standing idle because the equipment they need is somewhere else. Sortly exists because this problem demands a tool that lives where the work happens — in the crew member's pocket, not on a warehouse manager's desktop. A system that's as fast as scanning a barcode and as intuitive as taking a photo, because field crews will adopt something that's faster than calling the boss, and they'll ignore everything else.

## Market Frame of Reference

### The Category We're Defining
**Category:** Mobile inventory tracking for field service teams
**Why this frame:** The obvious frame would be "inventory management software" — the broad category that includes retail inventory, warehouse management, and ERP modules. That frame is wrong for this buyer because it activates the wrong competitive set and the wrong evaluation criteria. A plumbing company owner searching for "inventory management" gets results designed for retailers and warehouse operators — tools that assume stationary inventory, SKU-heavy catalogs, and desktop-first workflows. None of that matches their reality.

By framing as "mobile inventory tracking for field service," Sortly activates the right evaluation criteria: Can my crew use this from a truck? Can I see what's on each vehicle? Is setup fast enough that I'm not spending a week cataloging before I get value? This frame also narrows the competitive set to the alternatives this buyer actually weighs: their current approach (spreadsheet, memory, shouting across the job site), a general-purpose inventory app they found on the App Store, or doing nothing. It excludes the alternatives that aren't real competitors for this buyer: ERP modules, warehouse management systems, and enterprise asset management platforms.

The risk of this frame is that it sounds niche — and it is. That's the point. A plumbing company owner should land on Sortly's presence and immediately think "this was built for someone like me." A retail store owner should think "this isn't for me." That's the positioning working.

### How Buyers Evaluate Alternatives
Field service owners and operations managers approach this problem practically, not analytically. They don't run RFPs or build evaluation matrices. They Google something like "track tools on trucks" or "inventory app for construction," try whatever looks simplest, and decide within a week. Their evaluation happens in this order:

First, they check if it works on a phone — because their crews are in the field, not at desks. If the product requires a desktop to be useful, it's eliminated in 30 seconds.

Second, they check if it's simple enough for their least tech-savvy crew member. The buyer isn't evaluating for themselves — they're evaluating for the 55-year-old plumber who grudgingly uses a smartphone for dispatch and nothing else. If it looks complicated, the buyer knows adoption will fail.

Third, they check if they can see results fast — add a few items, take some photos, scan a barcode, and feel the "aha" of knowing where something is. If setup requires a week of data entry before value appears, they'll abandon the trial.

Price is checked last, and only matters if it passes the first three tests. At the $50-$500/month range, price is a minor factor relative to the pain it solves. The buyer's mental math isn't "can I afford this?" — it's "is this worth more than the stuff I lose every month?"

**What buyers consider and why:**

**"We've been using a spreadsheet / we just keep track in our heads"**
This is where 80%+ of the target segment lives today. Buyers stick with it because it's free, familiar, and "good enough" — until it isn't. The system works when the owner can personally see and touch everything. It breaks down when the company grows past 3-5 trucks, because the owner's mental inventory can't scale. Buyers don't leave this approach because they want better technology — they leave because a specific, painful event (lost $3,000 tool, crew showed up without equipment, audit revealed $20K in missing inventory) proves the current approach is costing real money.

**"There are a bunch of inventory apps — what about [generic app]?"**
Buyers find general-purpose inventory apps through App Store searches. These apps are often free or cheap and offer basic list-and-photo functionality. What attracts buyers: low cost, quick start. What makes them walk away: no multi-user check-out tracking, no QR/barcode scanning workflow, no reporting that shows patterns over time, no integrations with accounting tools. The buyer realizes they need a system, not just a list.

**"We looked at InFlow / a 'real' inventory system"**
Some buyers — usually the more operationally sophisticated ones, or those with a warehouse component — evaluate InFlow or similar. They like the depth: purchase orders, supplier management, multi-warehouse tracking. What makes them walk away: it's built for product inventory, not field assets. The mobile experience is secondary to the desktop experience. Setup requires mapping SKUs, categories, and warehouse locations before value appears. For a field service company that just needs to know "which truck has the pipe threader?", InFlow is solving the wrong problem in the wrong way.

**"We could just buy more tools / absorb the cost"**
This is the "do nothing" alternative dressed in pragmatism. Buyers rationalize: "Tools break and get lost — that's just the cost of doing business." This holds until the aggregate becomes visible — usually at an annual audit or when the CFO flags material costs trending up. The realization that changes the calculus: "We're not losing tools because the work is hard. We're losing them because nobody can see what we have."

## Value Wedge
Sortly's value wedge is **crew-adopted visibility** — inventory tracking that actually gets used by field crews because it's built for how they work, not how a warehouse operator works. The edge isn't any single feature (photos, QR codes, mobile app). The edge is that the entire product was designed around a 10-second interaction from a truck cab or a job site: scan it, snap it, move on. This means the data is actually current — because the people touching the inventory are the ones updating it, in real time, as part of their natural workflow.

This is what a happy customer would say to a peer: "We got Sortly and now I actually know where everything is. My guys scan stuff in and out — it takes them five seconds — and I can see it all from my phone. I stopped getting calls at 6 AM asking where the generator is."

**Why this wedge holds:**
- **Buyers perceive it as meaningful:** The #1 objection in this segment isn't about features — it's "will my crew actually use it?" Sortly's simplicity directly addresses the buyer's deepest concern. When they see the scan-and-go workflow, the reaction is recognition: "okay, even my most stubborn guy could do that."
- **It's ownable:** Competitors either optimize for desktop/warehouse workflows (InFlow, Fishbowl) or optimize for enterprise IT asset tracking (Asset Panda, GoCodes). None have built their entire UX around the field crew member scanning from a truck. Moving into this position would require them to rebuild their core product assumptions — you can't bolt "crew-friendly" onto a desktop-first architecture.
- **It resonates on contact:** The test is whether the buyer, within 60 seconds of seeing Sortly, can picture their crew using it. The photo-based, visual interface creates immediate comprehension — there's no abstraction layer between "here's my tool" and "here it is in the system." The gap between physical inventory and digital record collapses to a photo and a scan.

## Positioning Statement
> For field service owners and operations managers running 5-50 trucks who are tired of being the human inventory system, Sortly is the mobile inventory tracking platform that gives every crew member real-time visibility into what's on every truck and every job site. Unlike spreadsheets that nobody updates, generic inventory apps that don't support field workflows, and enterprise systems that crews will never adopt, Sortly was built for the 10-second scan-and-go reality of field service — which is why crews actually use it and the data stays current.

**How to use this statement:**
This statement is a compass, not copy. Marketing uses it to frame campaign themes: lead with the "human inventory system" pain, resolve with "crews actually use it." Sales uses it to anchor the first 30 seconds: "Most field service owners I talk to tell me they're basically the company's inventory system — they're the one who knows where everything is. That's what Sortly fixes." Product uses it to evaluate roadmap priorities: does this feature make the 10-second scan-and-go experience faster and simpler, or does it add complexity that threatens crew adoption? If it threatens adoption, it threatens the wedge.
