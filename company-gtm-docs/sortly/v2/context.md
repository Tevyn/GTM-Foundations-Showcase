# GTM Foundations — Shared Context

## Company Overview
Sortly is cloud-based, mobile-first inventory management software designed for visual tracking with photo-based item documentation, barcode/QR scanning, alerts, reporting, and team collaboration. The product spans iOS, Android, and web with offline mobile access and real-time sync. Key features include item entry with photos, bulk import, custom fields, folder organization, tagging, role-based permissions, barcode/QR scanning, custom label printing, pick lists, purchase orders, check-in/check-out tracking, low stock alerts, date-based alerts, inventory summary reports, activity history, and automated email report subscriptions. Sortly integrates with QuickBooks, Slack, Teams, plus API/webhooks. It operates on a freemium model with tiered plans up to Enterprise, serving 15,000+ customers including Schneider IT, Sephora, Chewy, and Terminix across 28+ industries. The product is positioned as the easy alternative to spreadsheets and heavier inventory systems like InFlow.

## ICP Context (added by ICP skill)

### Pain Patterns (for PSF to quantify and map to solutions)
- **"Where's the [tool/equipment]?" syndrome:** Field crews can't locate high-value assets across trucks, job sites, and warehouses. Owner becomes human inventory system. → Business consequence: 30-90 min per search event of owner/manager time, emergency rentals/purchases at premium pricing, daily drain on profitability at 5+ trucks.
- **Duplicate purchasing from invisible inventory:** Crews buy parts and consumables they already have because no visibility across trucks. → Business consequence: compounds to thousands/year in wasted material spend, inflated P&L costs, root cause invisible without a single source of truth.
- **Job site delays from missing/unavailable equipment:** Crews arrive without right tools/parts, job stalls. → Business consequence: idle crew labor at $75-$150/hr, customer reputation damage, project timeline slippage, cascading schedule disruption.
- **No check-out/check-in accountability:** Tools move between crews with no record, no audit trail. → Business consequence: loss rates stay high, no behavioral incentive to return items, no pattern identification possible, preventable loss absorbed as fixed cost.
- **Manual inventory audits as dreaded ordeal:** 1-3 days pulling crews off billable work, error-prone, results immediately stale. → Business consequence: lost billing revenue during audit, surprise write-offs, companies delay audits which compounds discrepancy.

**Buyer outcomes by role:**
- Champion (Owner/Ops Manager): Wants to stop being the human inventory system. Needs visibility without asking. Cares about reducing tool loss and job delays.
- Economic Buyer (Owner/CFO): Needs P&L impact — reduced replacement costs, reduced duplicate purchasing, fewer audit write-offs.
- Execution Owner (Field Supervisor/Lead Tech): Needs it to be faster than the current method. 10-second scan-and-go from a phone. Zero friction.

**Initial impact magnitude estimates for PSF:**
- Tool/equipment loss: $5K-$50K/year for companies in this segment depending on fleet size
- Duplicate purchasing: $3K-$15K/year in unnecessary material spend
- Job delays from missing equipment: $10K-$50K/year in unbilled labor and schedule disruption
- Audit downtime: $5K-$20K/year in lost billable time plus write-off surprise

**Qualification signals (severity/urgency/frequency):**
- High severity: $10K+ write-offs, 5+ hrs/week tracking equipment, recent job delay from missing tools
- High urgency: Recently added trucks/crews, painful audit just completed, high-value asset lost recently, new ops manager hired
- High frequency: Weekly occurrences, monthly duplicate purchases, multiple crew leads complaining

### Competitive & Market Context (for Positioning)
- **Primary alternative:** Spreadsheets, paper, memory — used by 80%+ of this segment. Works until crew count exceeds owner's visibility. Sortly wins on mobile-first simplicity.
- **InFlow Inventory:** Direct competitor, but more warehouse/retail/SKU-oriented and desktop-heavy. Sortly wins for field service on mobile UX and photo-based tracking.
- **Asset Panda / GoCodes:** Purpose-built asset tracking, but target larger companies with higher price points and IT/fixed-asset focus. Sortly wins on ease of use and SMB price.
- **Doing nothing:** Absorbing loss as cost of doing business. Hardest to beat — requires making the cost visible. Trigger events crack this inertia.
- Adjacent category confusion: Sortly could be mistaken for retail inventory / POS-adjacent tools (Cin7, Shopify Inventory) or enterprise asset management (SAP, Oracle). Neither is the right frame for field service.

### Buyer Language & Framing (for Messaging)
- Buyers say "what's on each truck" — Sortly says "cloud-based inventory management." Lead with trucks and crews, not clouds.
- Buyers describe "losing tools" and "nobody knows who had it" — frame accountability gap as people problem, not feature.
- Biggest buyer objection: "my guys won't use anything complicated" — adoption by least-tech-savvy crew member is the bar.
- Buyers feel the time cost personally ("spent my Saturday doing inventory") — message to the personal time impact.
- Buyers name the symptom ("keep buying stuff we already have") not the capability ("real-time visibility"). Name the symptom first.
- Content themes: tool tracking for contractors, equipment management for field service, "stop losing tools," "know what's on every truck," inventory tracking without spreadsheets.
- Buyer motivation: control, visibility, and "not having to be the one who knows where everything is."

## PSF Context (added by PSF skill)

### For Positioning
- **Solution differentiation angles:**
  - Spreadsheets/paper address the "list" need but fail because they can't be updated from the field in real time. Sortly's mobile-first, photo-based approach embeds the update into the workflow — scan when you grab it, scan when you return it — which is why adoption sticks where spreadsheet discipline didn't.
  - InFlow and heavier inventory tools address warehouse/SKU tracking but fail for field service because they're desktop-oriented and assume a stationary inventory model. Sortly's approach assumes inventory is distributed and moving — trucks, job sites, warehouses — which matches this ICP's reality.
  - Asset tracking tools (Asset Panda, GoCodes) address tagging and tracking but fail for SMB field service because they're priced and designed for enterprise IT/fixed-asset workflows. Sortly's ease-of-use and price point meet SMB field service where they are.
- **Problem areas ranked by right-to-win:**
  1. Asset location blindness — strongest fit. Mobile photo inventory with QR scanning is directly purpose-built for this. No alternative matches this for field crews.
  2. Job site delays from equipment unavailability — strong fit. Check-in/check-out tracking and real-time status are core Sortly features that directly resolve this.
  3. Inventory bleed (duplicate purchasing / silent loss) — solid fit. Visibility across all locations addresses root cause, though the full purchasing workflow (PO management, approval chains) isn't Sortly's core.
- **Win/lose scenarios:**
  - Sortly wins when: buyer is replacing spreadsheets/memory, primary pain is field visibility, crews already use smartphones, buyer values speed-to-value over feature depth.
  - Sortly loses when: buyer needs full PO/purchasing workflow, buyer needs ERP-grade reporting, buyer's primary use case is warehouse SKU management, or the company is too small (1-2 trucks) for the pain to be acute.
- **Objections that reveal positioning opportunities:**
  - "My guys won't use it" → Opportunity to position on crew-friendly simplicity vs. enterprise complexity.
  - "We tried a spreadsheet and it failed" → Opportunity to position on mobile-embedded workflow vs. separate administrative task.
  - "The amounts are too small" → Opportunity to position on aggregate invisible cost vs. visible line items.

## Positioning Context (added by Positioning skill)

### For Messaging
- **Market category and frame of reference:** "Mobile inventory tracking for field service teams" — not generic "inventory management software." This frame activates the right evaluation criteria (works on phone, crew-simple, fast setup) and excludes wrong competitors (ERP, warehouse systems, retail inventory). Buyers should immediately think "this was built for someone like me."
- **Value wedge in buyer language:** "Crew-adopted visibility" — inventory tracking that actually gets used by field crews because it's built for a 10-second scan-and-go interaction from a truck or job site. Happy customer version: "My guys scan stuff in and out — it takes five seconds — and I can see it all from my phone. I stopped getting calls at 6 AM asking where the generator is."
- **Competitive contrast themes:**
  1. "Human inventory system vs. system in everyone's pocket" — the owner as bottleneck vs. distributed, real-time visibility
  2. "Desktop-first vs. truck-cab-first" — warehouse-designed tools vs. field-designed tools
  3. "Setup project vs. instant value" — enterprise onboarding vs. scan-and-go simplicity
- **Positioning statement as messaging spine:** "For field service owners and ops managers running 5-50 trucks who are tired of being the human inventory system, Sortly is mobile inventory tracking that gives every crew member real-time visibility." Messaging should anchor on the "human inventory system" pain and resolve with "crews actually use it" — never lead with features or technology.
- **Buyer evaluation criteria ranked:** (1) Works on a phone in the field, (2) Simple enough for least-tech-savvy crew member, (3) Fast time to first value (minutes, not weeks), (4) Price justified by visible pain. Messaging should lead with criteria #2 (crew adoption) because that's where the strongest anxiety lives.
- **Evidence anchors for credibility:** Customer tracking $1M+ equipment across 50+ locations. 60-80% reduction in time spent locating assets. 10-20% material cost reduction in first year. 40-70% reduction in job site delays. Payback period under one month.
- **"Where we don't win" guidance:** Don't compete on PO/purchasing workflow depth (InFlow is stronger). Don't compete on enterprise asset lifecycle (Asset Panda's territory). Don't compete on warehouse SKU management (wrong use case). Steer conversations toward field visibility, crew adoption, and the scan-and-go workflow — that's where the wedge is sharpest.
