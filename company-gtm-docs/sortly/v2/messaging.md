# Sortly — Messaging Framework

## Positioning Spine
> For field service owners and operations managers running 5-50 trucks who are tired of being the human inventory system, Sortly is the mobile inventory tracking platform that gives every crew member real-time visibility into what's on every truck and every job site. Unlike spreadsheets that nobody updates, generic inventory apps that don't support field workflows, and enterprise systems that crews will never adopt, Sortly was built for the 10-second scan-and-go reality of field service — which is why crews actually use it and the data stays current.

**How this anchors the messaging:** Every message below traces back to two anchors from this statement: the pain ("being the human inventory system") and the resolution ("crews actually use it and the data stays current"). The narrative arc leads with the pain, the pillars explain why Sortly resolves it, and the voice guidance ensures the language stays grounded in field service reality. When evaluating any piece of copy or talk track, test it against this spine: does it reinforce the idea that the owner stops being the bottleneck because crews adopt the system? If not, it's drifting.

## The Story

### The Problem We're Solving
You started your company to build things, fix things, or keep buildings running — not to be a dispatcher for misplaced tools. But somewhere between your third truck and your eighth, you became the person who knows where everything is. Your phone rings before 7 AM: "Where's the pipe threader?" "Who has the generator?" "Did we leave the saw at the Elm Street job?" You spend five, eight, ten hours a week answering questions nobody else can answer, because the information about what's on each truck, at each site, and in the warehouse lives in your head — and nowhere else. Meanwhile, your crews are buying things you already own because they can't see what's on other trucks, showing up to job sites without the right equipment because nobody checked, and losing tools that won't surface until the next audit. The money leaks out in small amounts — a duplicate purchase here, a rental there, an idle crew waiting for a tool — and by the end of the year, it adds up to $30K, $50K, $80K that never shows up on a single line item.

### Why Now
This problem gets worse, not better, with growth. Every truck you add, every crew you hire, every new job site you take on stretches the gap between what you own and what you can see. The approaches that got you here — the spreadsheet on your laptop, the whiteboard in the office, the "just call me" system — don't scale past the point you've already passed. And the longer you wait, the more the invisible costs compound: more duplicate purchases, more lost tools absorbed as "normal," more Monday mornings scrambling to figure out who has what. The field service companies that fix this early grow without the drag. The ones that don't keep bleeding margin as they scale.

### Our Promise
When every crew member can scan a barcode and see what's on every truck in ten seconds, you stop being the human inventory system. You know where your equipment is without asking. Your crews stop buying things you already own. Your jobs start on time because the right tools are on the right truck. And the time you get back — the hours you spent fielding calls and driving between sites and counting tools on a Saturday — goes back into running and growing the business you built.

## Messaging Pillars

### 1. Crew-Adopted Visibility — "Your crews will actually use this. That's the whole point."
The reason spreadsheets and "better processes" fail in field service is that they depend on people doing extra work at the end of a long day. Sortly is built for a different reality: scan a barcode from the truck, snap a photo if it's new, and move on. Ten seconds. Crews adopt it because it's faster than calling the boss to ask where something is — and once they do, you have real-time visibility into every tool, every truck, every site without asking anyone.

**Why this matters:** This pillar leads because it addresses the #1 objection and the #1 evaluation criterion for this buyer: "Will my guys actually use it?" Every field service owner has tried some version of "let's be more organized" and watched it fail because the system added friction to the field crew's day. Sortly's scan-and-go design isn't a nice-to-have feature — it's the structural reason the data stays current, which is the structural reason visibility works. Without crew adoption, no inventory system delivers value. This is the wedge.
**Proof point:** Field service companies using Sortly report 60-80% reduction in time spent locating equipment within the first month — because the data is current, because the crews are the ones updating it.

### 2. Truck-Level Visibility — "Know what's on every truck without making a single call."
Every tool, every piece of equipment, every bin of parts — searchable by truck, by job site, by warehouse, from your phone. When a crew lead asks "where's the generator?", you don't need to text three people and wait. You check Sortly. When you're dispatching Monday morning, you see exactly what's on each truck before anyone leaves the yard. Photo documentation means you see the item, not just a line in a database.

**Why this matters:** This pillar connects to the deepest pain point for this buyer — being the human inventory system. The owner's phone ringing at 6 AM isn't just a time cost; it's a signal that the business depends on one person's memory. Truck-level visibility is the functional resolution: the information moves from the owner's head to a shared, searchable system that every crew member can see. The ROI is immediate and personal — the owner gets their morning back.
**Proof point:** Customers track over $1M in equipment across 50+ locations with Sortly, replacing the "call the owner" system with self-serve search that takes seconds.

### 3. Invisible Cost Recovery — "Stop buying what you already own."
Most field service companies lose $15K-$50K a year to duplicate purchases, untracked tool loss, and idle crews waiting for equipment — but it never shows up on a single line item. It's a $200 fitting order here, a $400 tool rental there, a half-day of four workers standing around because the saw was on the wrong truck. Sortly makes the invisible visible: you see what you own, where it is, and who has it — so you buy less, lose less, and waste less.

**Why this matters:** This pillar converts the emotional pain ("this is frustrating") into economic urgency ("this is costing me $40K a year"). The owner knows something is off — material costs are high, tools keep disappearing — but can't diagnose it because the data doesn't exist. Sortly creates the data. The first audit after implementation typically surfaces $5K-$20K in inventory that was "missing" but actually sitting on the wrong truck or in a forgotten corner of the warehouse. That discovery alone often exceeds the annual cost of Sortly multiple times over.
**Proof point:** A construction customer identified $18K in duplicate inventory within the first 90 days, leading to purchasing changes that save an estimated $25K/year ongoing.

## Voice & Tone

### Voice Characteristics
| Characteristic | Sounds Like | Doesn't Sound Like |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Practical** | "Your crews scan a barcode, you see where everything is." | "Our AI-powered platform leverages visual inventory intelligence." |
| **Direct** | "You're losing $20K a year in tools you can't find. Here's how to fix it." | "Many organizations experience challenges with asset visibility across distributed teams." |
| **Peer-level** | "We've seen this pattern in hundreds of field service companies." | "As a leading provider of inventory management solutions..." |
| **Empathetic** | "You started this company to do the work, not to track where the work tools went." | "Inefficient inventory processes reduce organizational productivity." |

### Tone by Context
- **Website / marketing content:** Practical and specific. Lead with the pain ("still tracking tools in a spreadsheet?"), resolve with the outcome ("know what's on every truck"), and show the product doing it (photos, scanning, the mobile interface). Avoid abstraction — this buyer thinks in trucks and tools, not platforms and solutions.
- **Sales conversations:** Peer-to-peer and curious. The rep should sound like someone who's talked to hundreds of field service owners and understands the problem before the prospect describes it. Lead with "I talk to owners who tell me they spend half their week answering 'where is the [tool]' — does that ring true for you?" Not a pitch — a conversation with someone who gets it.
- **Customer success:** Grounded in results. Frame value in the buyer's terms: "Since your team started using check-in/check-out, your Monday morning equipment scrambles have dropped to near zero. Your guys are actually scanning — which means you're not getting those 6 AM calls anymore." Celebrate the operational win, not the feature usage.

### Buyer Language Patterns
| Buyers Say | We've Been Saying | Use Their Language |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "What's on each truck" | "Cloud-based inventory management" | Say "truck" and "job site," not "cloud" and "platform" |
| "My guys won't use anything complicated" | "Easy-to-use for businesses of all sizes" | Say "your guys" and "ten seconds" — name the crew and the speed, not the general ease |
| "We keep buying stuff we already have" | "Real-time visibility across all locations" | Name the symptom first ("duplicate purchases"), then the fix, never the feature name alone |
| "I spent my Saturday doing inventory" | "Inventory management from any device" | Acknowledge the personal time cost — "get your Saturday back" beats "manage from any device" |
| "Nobody knows who had it last" | "Activity history and check-in/check-out" | Frame as accountability and trust between crews, not as a feature name |

**The vendor test:** If you replaced "Sortly" with "InFlow" or "Asset Panda" or any generic inventory app and the copy still made sense, the messaging is too generic. Redo it. The copy should feel wrong with another company's name because it's anchored in field-crew adoption, truck-level visibility, and the scan-and-go workflow — things that are specifically Sortly's approach.

## Talk Track Guidance

### "What do you do?"
"We make inventory tracking that field service crews actually use. Most of the owners I talk to are basically their company's inventory system — they're the one who knows where every tool and piece of equipment is, and they spend hours a week answering 'where is the [thing]?' calls. Sortly puts a scannable, photo-based inventory in every crew member's pocket, so the owner stops being the bottleneck. Crews scan items in and out from their phone in about ten seconds. One of our customers went from losing track of $1M in equipment across 50 sites to knowing exactly where everything is — within the first month."

### "How are you different?"
"The biggest difference is that our crews actually use it — and that sounds simple, but it's the thing that makes everything else work. Most inventory tools were built for warehouses or desktops. They assume someone's sitting at a computer entering data. That doesn't work when your inventory is on eight trucks and three job sites and the people touching it are electricians, not administrators. We built everything around a 10-second interaction from a phone — scan a barcode, snap a photo, done. That's why the data is actually current, which is why you can actually trust what you see. Compared to a spreadsheet, we're what makes the spreadsheet approach actually work. Compared to InFlow or a heavier system, we're what your crews will actually adopt."

### Language to Avoid
- **"Platform" or "solution"** — these are vendor words. Say "app" or "system" or just "Sortly." A plumber doesn't buy a platform.
- **"Leverage," "utilize," "optimize"** — corporate-speak that signals "we don't understand your world." Say "use," "track," "see."
- **"Businesses of all sizes"** — the broadest possible non-claim. Say "field service companies" or "contractors" or "companies with trucks in the field."
- **"Digital transformation"** — no field service owner has ever wanted to digitally transform. They want to stop losing tools and getting calls at 6 AM.
- **"Inventory management" as the lead** — this is the category, not the value. Lead with the problem ("know what's on every truck") or the outcome ("stop being the human inventory system"), not the category label.
