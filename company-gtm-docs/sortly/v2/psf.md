# Sortly — Problem-Solution Fit

## Overview
This document maps Sortly's strongest problem-solution fit for field service companies (construction, plumbing, electrical, HVAC) with 10-100 employees managing assets and consumables across trucks and job sites. Three problem areas were selected based on quantified business impact, solution strength, and acute relevance to this ICP: (1) asset location blindness across distributed crews, (2) uncontrolled inventory bleed from invisible duplicate purchasing and loss, and (3) job site delays from equipment unavailability. These three share a common root cause — no real-time source of truth for what's owned, where it is, and who has it — which means Sortly's solution maps cleanly across all three without stretching into capabilities the product doesn't have.

## Problem Areas

### 1. Asset Location Blindness — "Where is the [thing]?"

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | The owner or operations manager of a 30-person plumbing company with 8 trucks gets three calls before 9 AM: "Where's the pipe threader?" "Who has the camera scope?" "Did we leave the generator at the Elm Street job?" Each call triggers a chain — text the crew leads, wait for responses, sometimes drive to a job site to physically check. The owner spends 5-10 hours per week as the human inventory database, answering questions nobody else can answer because the information exists only in their head. The emotional toll: they started a business to build things, not to be a dispatcher for misplaced equipment. Every call is a reminder that they're the bottleneck, and the business can't scale past their personal memory. |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a field service company with 8 trucks and $200K-$500K in tools and equipment: Owner/manager time spent locating assets: 5-10 hrs/week at a loaded cost of $75-$125/hr = **$19,500-$65,000/year** in owner time alone. Emergency equipment rentals when assets can't be found: 2-4 incidents/month at $150-$500/rental = **$3,600-$24,000/year**. Crew idle time waiting for located equipment to arrive: 2-3 hrs/week across all crews at $45-$75/hr loaded crew cost = **$4,680-$11,700/year**. Total annual cost of asset location blindness for an 8-truck company: **$27,780-$100,700/year**. At this scale, Sortly's annual cost ($600-$6,000) represents a 5-170x return. Even at the conservative end, the payback period is under one month. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | Sortly resolves asset location blindness by creating a photo-based, mobile-accessible inventory that every crew member can update from the field in seconds. Each item gets a photo, a location (which truck, which job site, which shelf), and a QR/barcode label that crew members scan to check out or move. The owner stops being the human database because the database is now in everyone's pocket. This addresses root cause — not "the owner needs a better memory" but "the information needs to live somewhere accessible to everyone" — which is why a shared spreadsheet fails (nobody updates it from the field) and why a desktop system fails (crews aren't at desks). |

**Objections:**
- "My guys won't use an app — they'll just ignore it." → Underlying concern: adoption by non-tech-savvy field workers. Response: Sortly's core interaction is point-phone-at-barcode-and-tap. It's faster than texting the boss to ask where something is. The adoption bar isn't "will they love using an app?" — it's "is scanning a QR code faster than calling the office?" For most crews, it is. Start with the crew leads (who already use phones for dispatch) and let adoption spread.
- "We tried a spreadsheet and nobody kept it updated." → Underlying concern: the problem isn't the tool, it's the behavior. Response: Spreadsheets fail because updating them requires leaving the field context — you have to open a laptop, find the file, type in changes. Sortly embeds the update into the workflow: scan when you grab it, scan when you return it. The update happens at the moment of use, not as a separate administrative task.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in field services segment] accurately tracks over $1M in equipment across 50+ locations using Sortly, replacing a manual tracking system that required full-time administrative staff.
- Sortly case studies report that field service customers reduce time spent locating equipment by 60-80% within the first month of adoption.

### 2. Uncontrolled Inventory Bleed — Duplicate Purchasing and Silent Loss

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | A 45-person electrical contractor discovers during their annual audit that they own 14 multimeters across their fleet — but can only locate 8. Meanwhile, purchase records show they bought 5 new ones this year because crews reported theirs as "missing." The CFO sees inflated material costs on the P&L but can't distinguish necessary purchases from duplicates. Consumables are worse: copper fittings, PVC, wire, connectors — ordered in bulk by each crew independently because nobody knows what's already stocked on other trucks or in the warehouse. The frustration: the money is leaking out in $50-$500 increments that are individually too small to investigate but collectively represent a significant margin hit. The controller says "our material costs are too high" but can't pinpoint why. |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a 45-person electrical contractor with $300K in annual material/parts spend: Duplicate purchasing of tools (items bought when existing ones can't be located): estimated at 8-15% of tool budget = **$4,000-$12,000/year** based on typical fleet tool inventories of $50K-$80K. Duplicate purchasing of consumables/parts (ordered per-crew without visibility into shared stock): estimated at 5-10% of consumables spend = **$10,000-$20,000/year**. Silent tool loss (items that disappear without triggering a replacement search — discovered only at audit): 3-8% of total tool value annually = **$1,500-$6,400/year**. Total inventory bleed: **$15,500-$38,400/year**. This compounds: without a tracking system, the leakage is invisible and therefore never addressed. Companies in this segment often absorb $20K+/year in preventable waste without realizing it because no single line item looks wrong — it's the aggregate that bleeds margin. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | Sortly resolves inventory bleed by making all inventory — across every truck, warehouse, and job site — visible in one searchable, photo-documented system with low-stock alerts and activity history. Before a crew orders new fittings, they can check Sortly to see what's already in stock across the organization. Before buying a replacement tool, they can search to see if the "missing" one is sitting on another truck. Custom QR labels on every item create an audit trail: who had it last, when it moved, where it went. This addresses root cause — not "crews are irresponsible" but "crews have no visibility into shared inventory" — and turns the fix into a structural system rather than a behavioral mandate. |

**Objections:**
- "The amounts are too small to justify a new system." → Underlying concern: each individual duplicate purchase is trivial. Response: The problem isn't any single purchase — it's the invisible aggregate. Ask: "What was your total tool and materials spend last year, and what percentage would you guess was duplicate or unnecessary?" Most owners estimate 3-5%. The real number, once tracked, is typically 8-15%. Even at the low estimate, that's a meaningful margin recovery for a $5M-$15M revenue company.
- "We already have purchase approval processes." → Underlying concern: they believe process solves visibility. Response: Approval processes prevent unauthorized spending but can't prevent authorized duplicate spending. When a crew lead says "I need a new pipe cutter," the approver has no way to know there are two already sitting in the warehouse or on another truck. Visibility comes before approval — you need to know what you have before you can decide what to buy.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in construction segment] reduced material waste by identifying $18K in duplicate inventory within the first 90 days of implementing Sortly, leading to a purchasing policy overhaul that saves an estimated $25K/year ongoing.
- Field service companies using Sortly report average material cost reductions of 10-20% in the first year, primarily from eliminated duplicate purchasing and reduced loss write-offs.

### 3. Job Site Delays from Equipment Unavailability

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | A general contractor's framing crew arrives at 7 AM to start a second-story addition. The framing nailer that was supposed to be on the truck was checked out by another crew yesterday and never returned. The crew lead calls the office. The office calls the other crew. The other crew is 45 minutes away. The framing crew sits idle for two hours — four workers at $35-$50/hour each — while the nailer makes its way across town. Meanwhile, the homeowner sees four workers standing in their driveway doing nothing. The project manager's frustration: this was completely preventable, but without a system to show equipment status (checked out, available, in repair), scheduling is just a guess. Every Monday morning is a scramble to figure out which truck has what, and inevitably, someone shows up short. |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a 25-person general contractor running 5 crews: Crew idle time from equipment unavailability: average 1 delay per crew per week, average 1.5 hours per delay, at loaded crew cost of $150-$300/hr (full crew) = **$39,000-$78,000/year** in unbilled labor. Revenue delay from project timeline slippage: each delay pushes completion by the same margin, deferring final payment. For a company billing $3M-$8M/year with 30-60 day payment terms, even small delays compound into cash flow impact. Emergency equipment procurement (rush delivery, rental, borrowed at premium): 3-5 incidents/month at $100-$400 = **$3,600-$24,000/year**. Customer satisfaction impact: each visible delay erodes trust, reduces referral likelihood, and in competitive markets, risks losing the next bid. Hard to quantify, but field service companies cite reputation as their #1 growth driver. Total quantifiable annual cost of equipment-driven delays: **$42,600-$102,000/year**. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | Sortly resolves equipment unavailability by making every asset's status — available, checked out (by whom, since when), in repair, at which location — visible to everyone with the app. Before dispatching a crew, the field supervisor checks Sortly to confirm the required equipment is available and on the right truck. Check-in/check-out tracking creates accountability: if a crew didn't return the framing nailer, it shows in the system. Low-stock alerts flag when consumables run below threshold so reordering happens before the shortage hits. Date-based alerts surface equipment due for maintenance before it fails on site. This addresses root cause — not "crews forget to return things" but "nobody can see equipment status in real time" — turning reactive scrambling into proactive dispatch planning. |

**Objections:**
- "We already plan our equipment allocation — this is a crew discipline issue." → Underlying concern: the problem is blamed on people, not systems. Response: Even the best crew leads can't remember the status and location of every piece of equipment across all jobs. Planning with a whiteboard or spreadsheet works when nothing changes — but in field service, everything changes daily. A crew keeps a tool an extra day. A job runs long. A rental gets returned early. Without real-time status, the plan is outdated before the first truck rolls.
- "We rent what we need — we don't need to track as much." → Underlying concern: rental-heavy companies feel less exposed to this pain. Response: Rental solves availability but at premium cost. If a company is renting because they can't locate owned equipment, they're paying twice — once for the asset they own and again for the rental they didn't need. Tracking what you own eliminates the rental-as-workaround pattern and reduces rental spend significantly.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in HVAC segment] eliminated Monday morning equipment scrambles by implementing Sortly's check-in/check-out system, reducing first-day-of-week delays from an average of 3 per week to near zero within 60 days.
- Sortly customers in field services report that real-time equipment visibility reduces job site delays by 40-70%, with the largest improvement coming from crews knowing equipment status before leaving the warehouse.

## GTM Workability Check
These three problem areas work across all GTM functions for this segment. **Marketing** can build campaigns around each — "Stop being the human inventory system," "How much is invisible inventory bleed costing your company?," "Your crew showed up. The equipment didn't." — because the pain is vivid, visual, and emotionally resonant with owner-operators. **Sales** can lead with any of these on a first call by describing the pain scenario (most owners will recognize it within the first sentence) and then moving to the quantified impact — the numbers are large enough relative to Sortly's price point to create immediate urgency. **Product** can prioritize against these: mobile scanning speed, check-out UX, low-stock alert reliability, and multi-location search are the capabilities that matter most, and roadmap decisions can be tested against "does this make any of our three core problems worse or better?" **CS** can measure success against all three — time spent locating assets (should drop), duplicate purchasing rate (should drop), equipment-related job delays (should drop) — giving QBRs clear, measurable value stories. The one area to watch: onboarding. The biggest risk is that crew adoption stalls because the initial inventory setup (photographing and labeling everything) feels like a project. CS should provide setup guidance that focuses on high-value items first, not comprehensive cataloging.
