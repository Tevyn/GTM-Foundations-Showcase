# GTM Foundations -- Shared Context

## Company Overview
Sortly is a cloud-based, mobile-first inventory management platform designed for businesses of all sizes. The product emphasizes visual tracking (photo-based item documentation), barcode/QR scanning, low stock alerts, reporting, and team collaboration features like check-in/check-out and role-based permissions. Sortly uses a freemium model with tiered plans up to Enterprise. They serve 15,000+ customers including Schneider IT, Sephora, Chewy, and Terminix across 28+ industries. They position themselves as the easy, visual alternative to spreadsheets and heavier inventory tools like InFlow. Key integrations include QuickBooks, Slack, Microsoft Teams, plus API/webhooks. Mobile apps available for iOS and Android with offline access.

## ICP Context (added by ICP skill)

### Pain Patterns (for PSF to quantify and map to solutions)
- Manual tracking (spreadsheets, paper) is time-consuming and error-prone -- businesses lose time on data entry and inventory counts
- No real-time visibility across locations -- teams don't know what's in stock where
- Spreadsheets can't scale with growing teams or inventory volume
- Check-in/check-out for shared tools/equipment is untracked, leading to losses
- No automated alerts for low stock or expiring items -- reactive instead of proactive
- Reporting and accountability gaps make audits painful
- Field teams lack mobile access to inventory data

### Competitive & Market Context (for Positioning)
- Primary alternative is spreadsheets and manual processes (pen and paper, Excel, Google Sheets)
- InFlow is the most commonly referenced competitor (positioned as a heavier, more complex tool)
- Other inventory/asset tracking tools exist across G2 category
- Sortly wins on ease of use and visual/mobile-first approach
- Loses when buyers need deep ERP integration or complex warehouse management

### Buyer Language & Framing (for Messaging)
- Buyers talk about "getting organized," "knowing what we have," and "not losing track of things"
- Case studies emphasize ease of use, accurate tracking, and smart reporting
- ROI language centers on efficiency, reduced costs, and streamlined operations
- Common phrases: "we outgrew our spreadsheet," "we need something our whole team can use," "we need to track things across locations"

## PSF Observations (added by PSF skill)
- Strongest use case is visual inventory tracking across locations -- the photo-based, mobile-first workflow is what the sales team demos and what case studies highlight most
- Tool/equipment check-in/check-out is a common use case but not deeply differentiated -- sign-out sheets are the main alternative
- Low stock and expiration alerts are a clear product strength but hard to quantify impact without customer data
- Pain is framed around efficiency and time savings, not connected to revenue or margin impact
- No quantified ROI data available beyond directional case study mentions ("tracks $1M+ in equipment," "50+ locations")
- Solution descriptions stay at the capability level -- what the product does, not why the approach is better than alternatives

## Positioning Observations (added by Positioning skill)
- Competes primarily against spreadsheets/manual processes and secondarily against InFlow and other inventory tools (Asset Panda, etc.)
- Wins on ease of use, visual/photo-based approach, and mobile-first design with offline access
- Loses when buyers need advanced warehouse management, manufacturing workflows, or deep ERP integration
- Positioning claim centers on "simple, visual, mobile-first inventory management for businesses of all sizes"
- Key perceived differentiators: photo-based tracking, mobile-first with offline, ease of setup (minutes not weeks), flexibility across 28+ industries
- Market category is "inventory management software" -- broad, undifferentiated category positioning
- No clear value wedge beyond ease of use -- "simple and visual" is the recurring theme but not defended as ownable

## Messaging Observations (added by Messaging skill)
- Key value props: "See Everything, Instantly" (visual tracking), "Track From Anywhere" (mobile-first), "Get Set Up in Minutes" (ease of use)
- Elevator pitch leads with product description ("visual, mobile-first inventory management app") rather than buyer problem
- Brand voice: Simple, Friendly, Confident -- adjectives only, no examples or application guidance
- Value props are feature-framed (photos, mobile app, easy setup) not problem-framed
- No narrative arc connecting buyer pain to urgency to resolution
- No talk track guidance, no objection handling, no buyer language integration
- Messaging is marketing-only -- a sales rep or CS team couldn't operationalize it
