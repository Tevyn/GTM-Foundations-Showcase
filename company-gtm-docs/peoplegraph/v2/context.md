# GTM Foundations — Shared Context

## Company Overview
PeopleGraph is a people analytics platform that unifies workforce data from HRIS (Workday), recruiting (Greenhouse/Lever), compensation (Radford/PayScale), and BI tools (Tableau/Looker). It provides predictive capabilities for attrition risk, headcount planning, compensation benchmarking, and manager effectiveness — purpose-built for high-growth tech companies where rapid scaling creates workforce planning pain that existing HR infrastructure can't handle.

## ICP Context (added by ICP skill)

### Pain Patterns (for PSF to quantify and map to solutions)
**Pain areas with business-consequence chains:**

1. **Unplanned attrition during scaling** → Employee loss → Replacement hiring delays → Understaffed teams → Delayed product roadmap or missed sales targets → Direct revenue impact

2. **Headcount planning inaccuracy** → Budget mismatch (over/under-hiring) → Cash burn escalation (over) or growth targets missed (under) → Board pressure and margin compression → Capital inefficiency

3. **Compensation equity blind spot** → Internally inconsistent pay discovered during exits → Retention of lower-paid talent → Attrition of better performers → Revenue impact and stalled diversity goals

4. **Manager quality variance** → High-performing managers retain teams; underperformers see 30% turnover → Leadership can't coach/replace without data → Talented ICs leave, replacement costs spike → Revenue disruption

5. **Data fragmentation across HR systems** → Manual reporting consuming 15+ hours/month → CHRO/ops spending time on data assembly vs. strategy → Slower decision cycles → Delayed scaling strategy

**Buyer outcomes by role:**
- CHRO/VP People: Reduce manual reporting, model scenarios quickly, lower attrition, identify pay gaps
- CFO: Headcount planning accuracy, attrition cost visibility, ROI on infrastructure investment
- Operating partner (VP Engineering/Sales): Visibility into their own team's attrition risk, manager effectiveness data for coaching

**Qualification signals indicating pain severity:**
- Unplanned departures (surprise resignations, not planned exits) = acute pain
- Growth targets 30%+ YoY = planning urgency signal
- Board-level compensation audit or ESG initiative = pay equity pain is real
- Recent manager turnover or skip-level feedback showing quality variance = manager effectiveness pain is acute

### Competitive & Market Context (for Positioning)

- Point solutions: Radford (compensation), Visier (attrition), separate BI tools for headcount

**Win/lose signals:**

**Differentiation angle for this segment:**
- All-in-one (attrition + compensation + planning) vs. multiple tools = faster time-to-value
- Built for rapid-growth dynamics (headcount doubling, team scaling, culture retention) vs. steady-state organizations
- Workday-native integration (not a separate platform) vs. bolt-on analytics layer

**Adjacent categories:**
- HR analytics, workforce intelligence, talent management, people data platform (all accurate; positioning should lean into "growth intelligence" for this segment)

## PSF Context (added by PSF skill)

### For Positioning

**Solution differentiation angles:**
- Attrition prediction: Typical approach (engagement surveys + historical trends) is reactive, misses people already interviewing. Our approach combines behavioral signals + market positioning + peer cohort data to identify at-risk employees 60+ days in advance, enabling proactive retention conversations before notice is given.
- Headcount planning: Typical approach (spreadsheet extrapolation + assumptions) breaks during rapid growth because attrition, hiring ramp, and comp all shift. Our approach models these dynamics together, enabling mid-year reforecasting and reducing planning error from ±15% to ±5%.
- Pay equity: Typical approach (annual audits + Radford reports + manual spreadsheet work) surfaces gaps months after they exist and requires 20+ hours of ops work to maintain. Our approach continuously monitors pay positioning and surfaces gaps in real-time, enabling proactive adjustment.

**Problem areas ranked by strength of right-to-win:**
1. **Unplanned attrition during scaling** (strongest): This is the #1 concern for CHRO and operating partners in rapid-growth companies. Predictive capability is genuinely differentiated vs. Workday analytics or engagement survey tools. Solution fit is high (data exists, capability is demonstrated, ROI is clear).
2. **Headcount planning accuracy** (strong): CFO is a powerful buyer with budget authority. Scenario modeling is a capability gap in Workday. ROI linkage to margin/cash burn is clear and compelling.
3. **Compensation equity** (medium-strong): Multiple buyers (CHRO + CFO + Recruiting), strong retention linkage, but slightly more commoditized (many tools do benchmarking). Differentiation is in integration + real-time updating vs. point solution audits.

**Win/lose scenarios by problem area:**
- Win on attrition: When customer has experienced recent unexpected departures, has dedicated people ops team, believes data can drive retention, and is skeptical of Workday analytics completeness.
- Lose on attrition to: Visier (if customer wants premium vendor), or to status quo if they believe engagement surveys are sufficient + attrition is not yet acute.
- Win on headcount planning: When CFO has been burned by planning misses, wants shared data infrastructure with CHRO, and sees modeling as critical to growth decision-making.
- Lose on headcount planning to: Custom build (if customer has strong data engineering team and believes internal tools are cheaper), or spreadsheet if growth and planning rigor aren't yet urgent.
- Win on pay equity: When board has pressured on DEI/pay transparency, or when company is experiencing attrition in underpaid cohorts. Also win when recruiting says "candidates want to know they're getting market rate."
- Lose on pay equity to: Annual consultant audits (if customer views this as one-time work), or to competitors (Visier) if buying broader platform.

**Objections that reveal positioning opportunities:**
- "Workday already has all this" → Positioning opportunity: Workday is infrastructure, we're the intelligence layer. They'll understand after seeing that Workday doesn't score attrition risk, model headcount scenarios, or flag pay gaps in real-time.
- "We tried predictive analytics before and it didn't work" → Positioning opportunity: Specificity matters. Generic ML models fail because they miss the domain signals (manager effectiveness, peer cohort comparison, market positioning). Our model is trained on people analytics data, not generic employee data.
- "This is nice-to-have after we finish Workday implementation" → Positioning opportunity: Reframe timing. They should deploy PeopleGraph at month 4–6 of Workday implementation (when data is stable), not after. Early deployment means they'll run their 18-month headcount plan with better forecasting.

## Positioning Context (added by Positioning skill)

### For Messaging
- **Market category and frame of reference:** "Predictive workforce intelligence for high-growth companies" — chosen to avoid the "people analytics" frame that activates Visier (premium, slow) or Workday analytics (perceived as sufficient). This frame shifts evaluation criteria to forward-looking capability: Can it predict attrition? Can it model headcount dynamically? Can it surface pay gaps in real-time?
- **Value wedge in buyer language:** "We were flying blind on attrition and headcount planning. PeopleGraph showed us who was at risk before they gave notice, and our CFO finally had a headcount model that adapted when reality changed." The wedge is predictive visibility during rapid growth — calibrated for companies doubling headcount where attrition patterns shift, planning assumptions break, and pay equity erodes faster than annual reviews catch.
- **Competitive contrast themes:** (1) "Retrospective reporting vs. predictive intelligence" — Workday tells you what happened, PeopleGraph tells you what's about to happen. (2) "Steady-state enterprise vs. growth-stage dynamics" — Visier is built for stable enterprises running annual planning cycles; PeopleGraph is built for companies where reality changes quarterly. (3) "Point solution audits vs. continuous monitoring" — Radford/PayScale give you a snapshot; PeopleGraph gives you a live feed.
- **Positioning statement as messaging spine:** "For CHROs and CFOs at high-growth tech companies facing workforce planning chaos, PeopleGraph is the predictive workforce intelligence platform that shows who's at risk, where plans are drifting, and where pay equity is eroding — before these become crises." Messaging should use this as an anchor without quoting it directly — translate the "before it becomes a crisis" frame into specific buyer scenarios.
- **Buyer evaluation criteria ranked:** (1) Predictive attrition accuracy — can it identify at-risk employees 60+ days before notice? (2) Headcount scenario modeling — can CFO and CHRO run "what if" models together? (3) Pay equity real-time monitoring — does it surface gaps continuously or just during audits? (4) Time to value — can we get insights in weeks, not months? (5) Integration with existing stack — does it work with Workday + Greenhouse + Tableau without requiring platform migration?
- **Evidence anchors for credibility:** Customer proof: 65% of at-risk cohort predicted 60+ days in advance; attrition dropped 3.5 points in year one; headcount forecast accuracy improved from ±15% to ±5%; pay equity adjustment ROI of 6.7x. Industry benchmarks: tech companies in rapid growth see 2-3 point attrition spikes in year 2; companies with predictive attrition visibility hold flat.
- **"Where we don't win" guidance:** Lose to Visier when buyer has $250K+ budget and wants comprehensive analytics suite for a stable enterprise. Lose to status quo when attrition hasn't yet spiked or when the company is still mid-Workday implementation. Lose to DIY when the company has a strong data engineering team and doesn't need answers urgently. Messaging should steer toward the urgency of rapid growth and the cost of waiting, not away from competitor strengths.
