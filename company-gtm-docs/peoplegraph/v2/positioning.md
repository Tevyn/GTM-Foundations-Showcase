# PeopleGraph — Positioning

## Overview
PeopleGraph is positioned for high-growth tech companies (Series B–C, 1,000–5,000 employees) where rapid scaling creates workforce planning crises that existing HR infrastructure can't prevent. The strongest right to win is in predictive attrition visibility — the ability to see who's at risk of leaving 60+ days before notice, when there's still time to intervene. This positions PeopleGraph not as another analytics dashboard on top of Workday, but as the intelligence layer that turns workforce data into forward-looking decisions during the most chaotic phase of a company's growth.

## Product Vision
Rapid growth breaks workforce planning. When a company doubles headcount in 18 months, the tools built for steady-state HR — Workday for record-keeping, engagement surveys for sentiment, spreadsheets for forecasting — can't keep pace. Leaders discover attrition after resignations, forecast headcount from last year's assumptions, and find pay gaps during exit interviews. PeopleGraph exists because scaling companies need predictive visibility, not retrospective reporting. The product unifies data from HRIS, recruiting, compensation, and BI systems into a single intelligence layer that shows what's about to happen — who's at risk, where headcount plans are drifting, where pay equity is eroding — so leadership can act before problems become crises.

## Market Frame of Reference

### The Category We're Defining
**Category:** Predictive workforce intelligence for high-growth companies

**Why this frame:** The obvious category is "people analytics," but that frame activates the wrong competitive set. In the buyer's mind, "people analytics" means Visier (expensive, enterprise-grade, 12-month implementation) or Workday's built-in reporting (already included, perceived as sufficient). Both frames disadvantage PeopleGraph — the first on price and brand, the second on inertia.

"Predictive workforce intelligence" reframes the evaluation around forward-looking capability rather than retrospective reporting. This shifts the competitive set: Workday analytics becomes a data source rather than a competitor ("Workday tells you what happened; we tell you what's about to happen"). Visier becomes a premium alternative for companies that want a broader analytics suite but don't need growth-specific modeling. And the real competitor — spreadsheets and manual planning — becomes visibly inadequate once the buyer evaluates through a predictive lens.

This frame also activates the right evaluation criteria for the ICP: Can it predict attrition before notice? Can it model headcount scenarios dynamically? Can it surface pay gaps in real-time? These are the questions that matter to a CHRO and CFO at a Series B company doubling headcount — not "does it have more dashboard widgets than Workday?"

### How Buyers Evaluate Alternatives
The CHRO or VP of People typically drives initial evaluation after a triggering event — a quarter with unexpectedly high attrition, a board conversation about headcount efficiency, or a pay equity audit that revealed gaps they didn't know existed. They look first at whether Workday can solve it (it usually can't, but this takes 2-4 weeks to confirm), then at whether their data team can build something (possible but slow and narrow), and finally at purpose-built solutions.

The CFO enters the evaluation when the conversation shifts to headcount planning accuracy or when the CHRO needs budget approval. The CFO evaluates on ROI clarity — they want to see the math on how better forecasting or reduced attrition translates to dollars saved or margin preserved.

**What buyers consider and why:**

**"Can't Workday handle this?"**
Buyers check Workday first because it's already deployed and the data is there. Workday's analytics show historical attrition rates, current headcount, and basic demographic reporting. What holds them back: Workday doesn't predict who's about to leave, doesn't model "what if attrition increases 3 points next quarter," and doesn't compare internal pay positioning against market in real-time. The CHRO realizes this after 2-4 weeks of trying to get the analytics team to build what they need in Workday — the data is there, but the intelligence layer isn't.

**"We could build this ourselves with Tableau/Looker"**
Data-literate companies consider internal builds because they already have BI tools and data engineering talent. The appeal is control and cost avoidance. What makes them walk away: building attrition prediction models requires domain-specific ML training data they don't have, maintaining headcount scenario models requires ongoing data engineering investment (2-3 FTEs), and the CHRO needs answers in weeks, not the 6 months an internal build would take. Companies that try this route typically get a basic attrition dashboard but never reach predictive capability.

**"What about Visier?"**
Visier is the established player in people analytics — strong brand, deep analytics, enterprise-grade. Buyers consider Visier when they want a comprehensive analytics platform and have the budget ($250K+ ACV) and timeline (9-12 month implementation) to support it. What makes them hesitate: Visier is built for steady-state enterprise analytics, not for the specific dynamics of rapid growth (doubling headcount, shifting attrition patterns, volatile comp benchmarks). Implementation timeline conflicts with the urgency of a company that needs answers now, not next year. And the cost premium is harder to justify when the primary need is predictive attrition and headcount planning, not a full analytics suite.

**"Let's just keep using spreadsheets and engagement surveys"**
The most common alternative is doing nothing differently — relying on the same spreadsheet-based planning, annual compensation reviews, and quarterly engagement surveys that got them to this point. This works when growth is steady and attrition is predictable. It breaks down when headcount doubles and the planning assumptions from 12 months ago no longer hold. The realization that tips buyers over: a mid-year reforecast that reveals they're 150 people off plan, or an exit interview that surfaces a pay gap they didn't know existed.

## Value Wedge
PeopleGraph's edge is predictive visibility during rapid growth — the ability to see workforce risks (attrition, planning drift, pay inequity) before they become crises, specifically calibrated for the dynamics of companies that are doubling headcount. This isn't a feature; it's an approach. Where Workday reports on what already happened and Visier provides analytics breadth for stable enterprises, PeopleGraph is built around the assumption that rapid growth changes everything — attrition patterns shift, planning assumptions break, and pay equity erodes faster than annual reviews can catch. The product is designed for that specific reality.

A happy customer would describe it this way: "We were flying blind on attrition and headcount planning. PeopleGraph showed us who was at risk before they gave notice, and our CFO finally had a headcount model that adapted when reality changed. It's the thing that made scaling feel manageable instead of chaotic."

**Why this wedge holds:**
- **Buyers perceive it as meaningful:** CHROs and CFOs at Series B-C companies consistently report that their #1 workforce concern is "we don't see problems until it's too late." Predictive visibility directly addresses the thing that keeps them up at night during growth — the feeling of being caught off-guard by departures, planning misses, and pay gaps.
- **It's ownable:** Workday's architecture is built for system-of-record, not predictive intelligence — adding ML-driven attrition scoring would require fundamental platform changes. Visier could build this but their go-to-market targets large enterprise in steady state, not high-growth mid-market. DIY builds can't match the domain-specific training data and model accuracy. The intersection of "predictive" + "growth-stage dynamics" is a position competitors haven't occupied.
- **It resonates on contact:** When reps say "we predict who's at risk of leaving 60 days before notice," the buyer reaction is recognition — "yes, that's exactly what I need" — not confusion. The value proposition maps directly to a pain they're already experiencing and have been unable to solve with existing tools.

## Positioning Statement
> For CHROs and CFOs at high-growth tech companies facing the workforce planning chaos of doubling headcount, PeopleGraph is the predictive workforce intelligence platform that shows who's at risk, where plans are drifting, and where pay equity is eroding — before these become crises. Unlike Workday analytics (retrospective) or Visier (built for steady-state enterprise), PeopleGraph is purpose-built for the dynamics of rapid growth, where attrition patterns shift quarterly, planning assumptions break mid-year, and compensation benchmarks move faster than annual reviews can track.

**How to use this statement:**
Marketing uses the "predictive visibility during rapid growth" frame to anchor campaigns — every piece of content should connect back to the idea that scaling companies need forward-looking intelligence, not backward-looking reports. Sales uses it in the first 30 seconds of a conversation to establish that PeopleGraph is different from Workday analytics and to reframe the evaluation criteria around prediction, not reporting. Product uses it to evaluate roadmap priorities: features that improve prediction accuracy or speed-to-insight for growth-stage dynamics are on-strategy; features that add reporting depth for steady-state analytics are off-strategy.
