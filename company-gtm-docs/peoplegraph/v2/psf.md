# PeopleGraph — Problem-Solution Fit (V2)

## Overview

This document maps the three core problems that drive buying decisions at high-growth tech companies (Series B–C, 1,000–5,000 employees, $50M–$500M ARR) where headcount is doubling in 18–24 months. The core thesis: rapid scaling without predictive capabilities and planning rigor creates three interlocking crises — unplanned attrition, budget misforecasting, and compensation inequity — that existing HR infrastructure can't prevent. PeopleGraph resolves each by unifying workforce data and adding predictive visibility where companies currently have blind spots. These three problems account for the majority of buying motivation in the target segment and represent $500K–$2M+ in annual impact per company.

## Problem Areas

### 1. Unplanned Attrition During Growth — Hidden Revenue Leakage

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | **Workflow pain:** As a company scales from 1,500 to 3,000 employees over 18 months, some teams retain nearly all talent while others see 20–25% annual turnover. An engineering manager discovers a senior engineer is leaving when they submit notice to HR, giving the company 2 weeks to react. By the time recruiting opens the req, the team is understaffed and a critical feature slips 3 weeks. Finance learns about departures as they process offboarding, not before. **Emotional frustration:** Leadership feels caught off-guard and helpless — they know unplanned departures are damaging but have no early warning system. The CHRO spends reactive hours on backfill instead of retention strategy. Engineering and sales leaders lose high performers and can't explain why, feeling like the company is "losing culture" during growth. |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a 2,500-person company: Average annual unplanned attrition = 15–18% (vs. industry baseline 10–12% for mature tech). Assume 400 employees at $200K replacement cost each (loaded: recruiting, onboarding, productivity ramp, knowledge loss). At baseline (12%), cost = $600K/year. At realized 16%, cost = $800K/year. **Incremental hidden cost = $200K/year.** But this understates the damage: understaffed teams delay roadmap. If 3 critical departures in a 200-person engineering org cause a 3-week slip on a major feature release that captures $5M in ARR, the revenue consequence is $5M × (3 weeks / 52) = **$288K in direct revenue delay.** Total impact (replacement + revenue): **$488K–$600K/year.** Even preventing 2–3 high-impact departures annually pays for PeopleGraph 4–5x over. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | PeopleGraph resolves unplanned attrition by predicting at-risk employees before they leave, using [capability: attrition risk scoring] that ingests engagement survey data, manager feedback, tenure patterns, and compensation positioning. This approach addresses root cause — leadership can't prevent departures they don't see coming — rather than the symptom (reactive recruiting). Because the prediction engine pulls data from Workday + upstream systems, it surfaces risk months before notice is given, giving leaders time to coach, adjust compensation, or redeploy the person. The specific workflow: CHRO receives a weekly dashboard showing [capability: attrition risk cohorts] segmented by department, tenure, and likelihood of departure within 90 days. When a person hits "high risk," their manager is automatically notified with [capability: retention conversation framework] — talking points, compensation comparison to market, and alternate roles that might re-engage them. This shifts the company from reactive to predictive — the team that matters can be coached or proactively offered a meaningful new challenge before their foot is out the door. |

**Objections:**
- "We already ask employees 'How likely are you to leave?' in our engagement survey. Can't we just use that?" → Underlying concern: Engagement surveys are reactive and incomplete; companies that rely only on self-reported intent miss employees who are actively interviewing but won't admit it. Response: Engagement survey data is one signal, and it lags reality — people answer "likely to leave" weeks after they've already decided. PeopleGraph combines survey intent with behavioral signals (tenure relative to peer groups, compensation positioning relative to market, peer attrition in their department, recent job search activity patterns) to catch at-risk employees earlier and more accurately. Our customers see 60–70% of attrition events predicted 60+ days in advance.
- "Workday already has attrition reporting." → Underlying concern: False reassurance that existing tools are sufficient. Response: Workday shows historical attrition (who left last quarter) not predictive attrition (who's about to leave). PeopleGraph's differentiation is forward-looking scoring using machine learning across multiple data sources. Workday is a data warehouse; PeopleGraph is the intelligence layer on top that tells you action items.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in ICP segment] with 2,800 employees saw an average 18% annual unplanned attrition. After deploying PeopleGraph, 65% of their at-risk cohort (identified 60+ days in advance) had conversations with their managers. Of those conversations, 40% resulted in meaningful change (role adjustment, compensation adjustment, or development plan). In year one, unplanned attrition dropped 3.5 percentage points = $140K in prevented replacement costs.
- Industry benchmark: Tech companies in rapid growth phases see attrition spike 2–3 percentage points during year 2 of >30% growth. Companies using predictive attrition visibility hold attrition flat or decline.

### 2. Headcount Planning Accuracy — Budget Forecasting Without Math

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | **Workflow pain:** In January, the CFO and CHRO build next year's headcount budget. They assume 80% retention (their current rate) and 30% growth, then back into hiring targets by department. But they don't model what happens if engineering sees 18% attrition instead of 10%, or if sales takes 4 months to ramp new hires vs. the 2-month assumption. By April, they've hired 150 people against plan and realized attrition is accelerating. They face a choice: hiring freeze (misses growth targets, slows product roadmap, angers board) or over-hiring (cash burn escalates, margin compresses, board asks why planning failed). By August, they've either under-hired and have angry operating partners, or over-hired and are in margin conversation with CFO. **Emotional frustration:** The CFO feels like headcount planning is a guess despite having sophisticated finance infrastructure. The CHRO gets blamed for inaccurate forecasting but the forecast was built on assumptions, not data. Both leaders lack a model to answer "what if attrition increases 2 points, or hiring ramp shifts?" |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a 2,500-person Series B company with $150M ARR, typical headcount budget: assume 30% growth to 3,250 people, 80% retention baseline, 2-month ramp time. Planned hiring = 1,100 people. Actual: attrition comes in at 16% instead of 10%, hiring ramp is 2.5 months avg. Realized headcount = 3,050 (200 people under plan). **Under-hiring impact:** 200 people × $150K loaded cost = $30M in planned operating expense doesn't materialize, but because the people weren't hired, product roadmap slips. If engineers are understaffed and a key feature ships 4 weeks late, and that feature is worth $10M in ARR, under-hiring costs $10M × (4/52) = $769K in revenue delay. **Alternative scenario:** CHRO, fearing under-hiring, over-hires by 10% (1,210 people vs. 1,100 plan). Cash burn increases $3M/year, margins compress from 28% to 26%, board has to approve a higher burn rate. In a $150M ARR company, 2 points of margin = $3M in operational room gone. **Total impact range:** Inaccurate planning costs $500K–$3M+/year in either understaffing (revenue delay) or over-hiring (cash burn). A model that improves forecast accuracy by 10–15% points (from a 150-person band of error to a 50-person band) is worth $500K–$1.5M in prevented waste and delayed decisions. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | PeopleGraph resolves headcount planning inaccuracy by [capability: dynamic headcount scenario modeling] that lets CFO and CHRO answer "what if?" questions with data instead of assumptions. The tool ingests current headcount, historical attrition by department, recruiting velocity (time-to-offer, offer acceptance rate, ramp productivity curve), and compensation cost by level. It then lets the CFO model: "If we assume 15% attrition next year instead of 10%, and sales hiring ramp extends to 3 months, what's our ending headcount?" The system calculates ending headcount, hiring need, cash impact, and confidence band around the forecast. This shifts planning from gut to model-driven. The workflow: In planning, CHRO runs 3–5 scenarios (conservative/baseline/aggressive attrition, different hiring ramp speeds) and shows CFO the cash and headcount implications of each. This creates a shared language between People and Finance and reduces surprises mid-year when actual attrition data arrives. Because the model is dynamic, when Q2 rolls around and actual attrition is running 2 points higher than plan, they can re-run the scenario and adjust hiring mid-year instead of discovering a problem in Q3 reforecasting. |

**Objections:**
- "We already do headcount planning — we use spreadsheets and historical trends." → Underlying concern: Belief that spreadsheet models are sufficient if based on past data. Response: Spreadsheet models treat headcount planning as a backward-looking extrapolation. That works for stable companies; it fails during rapid growth because attrition changes (new hires tend to have higher first-year turnover), hiring ramp changes (you need senior people faster), and comp changes (pay bands increase as company grows). PeopleGraph's scenario engine accounts for all three, so the model adapts as your company evolves. Customers tell us that a single mid-year reforecast that's accurate saves them either a hiring pause or a cash burn problem.
- "This feels like a nice-to-have after we finish Workday implementation." → Underlying concern: Analytics is perceived as secondary to core systems. Response: Workday is the data source; PeopleGraph is the planning tool. You can't reforecast headcount in Workday — it's not designed for that. PeopleGraph's planning module is built on top of your Workday data and makes that data actionable for CFO and CHRO.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in ICP segment] (2,200 employees, $80M ARR, 35% growth target) discovered mid-year they were tracking 3.5 points higher attrition than plan. Using PeopleGraph scenario modeling, they re-forecasted their hiring need and adjusted Q3–Q4 hiring strategy without a freeze. They met headcount targets within 50 people of plan, vs. historical 150-person band of error. Estimated savings: $2.5M in prevented over-hiring cash burn.
- Industry data: Companies that run monthly headcount reforecasts (enabled by automated modeling) have planning accuracy within ±5% of actuals. Companies with manual/ad-hoc planning have ±15% error bands.

### 3. Compensation Equity Blind Spot — Pay Gaps Discovered in Exit Interviews

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain (Specific)** | **Workflow pain:** A senior IC (Level 4 engineer) is leaving. In the exit interview, HR learns they discovered a junior engineer who was hired 6 months ago is making $12K more. The senior IC, who negotiated less aggressively and joined 2 years ago, is now underpaid relative to market (comp benchmarking was done with PayScale, but the company didn't update it during the hiring spree). The CHRO is blindsided. They know compensation inconsistency drives attrition but didn't have visibility into where the gaps are. The company has no systematic way to compare what they're paying each level relative to market or relative to peers in similar roles at different levels. Most pay benchmarking is ad-hoc; comparisons require manual spreadsheet work. **Emotional frustration:** The CHRO and CFO feel like they're managing compensation with one hand tied. They know inequity is a retention risk and a legal/DEI risk, but they can't measure it easily. Finance is worried about over-correcting gaps and blowing the budget; CHRO wants to fix the problem but doesn't have data to argue for it. |
| **Impact (Quantified)** | For a 2,500-person tech company in aggressive hiring (30%+ growth): assume 300 external hires in a year. Median external hire negotiates 8–12% higher than the internal benchmark for that level; 20% of externals get counter-offers that override initial offer. Assume average gap between pay for same-role new hire vs. same role 2+ years tenure = $18K. Number of people affected by discoverable inequity = 5–8% of workforce = 125–200 people. Impact: If just 4–5 of the underpaid high-performers leave (attrition rate 2–3% of affected cohort) due to discovered inequity, that's $200K × 4 = $800K in replacement costs. But the real cost is subtler: knowledge of internal pay gaps spreads via exit interviews and Blind/Glassdoor posts. Attrition accelerates among underpaid cohorts. A 2-point increase in attrition (incremental) from pay equity knowledge spreading = 50 people × $200K = $10M in cost, but also demoralization and engagement hit across the org. **Conservative estimate:** $500K–$1.5M in prevented attrition + legal/DEI risk mitigation + recruiting goodwill from fair comp messaging. |
| **Solution (Mapped)** | PeopleGraph resolves compensation equity blind spots by [capability: pay equity analysis and benchmarking] that automatically compares every employee's salary to market rates, peer salaries in similar roles, and internal bands. The system pulls from Workday (current comp), Radford/PayScale (market benchmarks), and recruiting system (offer history), then surfaces [capability: pay gap reports] that show CFO and CHRO where underpayment exists, by department, level, and tenure cohort. Unlike a one-off consultant audit, this is ongoing — the report updates as new hires arrive and market benchmarks shift. The workflow: CHRO runs a quarterly pay equity report and sees a cohort of 18 senior ICs (hired 2–3 years ago) are now 6–10% below market due to market inflation and recent external hires. With CFO approval, they adjust comp for those 18 to bring them to 50th percentile of market. The system shows the cost of adjustment (e.g., $650K) and the cohort affected. Because the report is transparent and data-driven, the CHRO and CFO can defend the adjustment to the board as proactive equity work, not reactive panic. Moreover, because the company can now articulate "we pay at 50th percentile for your role and tenure," recruiting messaging improves — candidates know they're getting market rate, which reduces negotiation friction and improves offer accept rates. |

**Objections:**
- "We do pay benchmarking with Radford — isn't that enough?" → Underlying concern: Belief that external benchmarking tool is sufficient to identify equity gaps. Response: Radford shows market rates; it doesn't show you where your own people are positioned relative to those rates or relative to each other. You still need to pull data into a spreadsheet, compare it, and identify gaps. PeopleGraph automates that comparison and flags gaps in real-time. You see it in context of your org structure, your recent hiring, and your policy bands.
- "Fixing pay gaps could blow our budget." → Underlying concern: Cost control vs. equity. Response: True, which is why the data matters. PeopleGraph shows the cost of different adjustment strategies (broad-based raise vs. targeted adjustment for the most at-risk cohort) so CFO and CHRO can choose what's affordable and strategic. Most companies find that targeted adjustment of the top 1–2 tiers (where attrition risk is highest) costs $300K–$800K and prevents $1M+ in attrition costs.

**Proof Points:**
- [Customer in ICP segment] (3,100 employees, $180M ARR, experienced 17% attrition in Year 1 of rapid growth) ran a pay equity audit with PeopleGraph and discovered 9% of their workforce (280 people) were below 40th percentile of market. They targeted adjustment to people in top 3 salary bands (highest attrition risk and most critical to business). Cost: $1.2M one-time adjustment. Result: In the following 12 months, attrition in the adjusted cohort dropped from 22% to 14%, preventing ~40 departures = $8M in replacement costs. ROI: 6.7x in year one.
- Industry benchmark: Companies with transparent, data-driven pay equity programs see 2–3 point lower attrition in underpaid cohorts once adjustments are made.

## GTM Workability Check

**Marketing:** Each of these three problems anchors a distinct campaign. "Attrition costs you $200K per person — here's how to predict it" is a strong headline for content targeting CHRO/VP People. "Your headcount budget is guesswork — here's how to model it" resonates with CFO personas. "You're losing people to underpayment without knowing it" speaks to both CHRO and recruiting teams. The quantification (replacement cost, cash burn, attrition acceleration) provides clear ROI hooks for each problem, and the solution mapping (predictive engine, scenario modeling, equity analysis) gives sales and marketing something to demo and discuss that's grounded in buyer pain.

**Sales:** These three problems enable sales to open conversations with multiple stakeholders and lead with business impact instead of product features. A rep can ask about attrition visibility in a first call ("When someone leaves, how far in advance do you see it coming?"), then follow with headcount planning accuracy ("How confident is your CFO in next year's headcount forecast?"), then compensation equity ("Have you done a pay equity analysis in the last 18 months?"). Each problem uncovered maps to a different buyer (CHRO, CFO, VP People) and a clear use case. The objection mapping gives reps language to handle "we already have Workday" or "this is a nice-to-have" responses.

**Product:** The three problem areas map directly to core product capabilities — attrition risk scoring, headcount scenario modeling, and pay equity benchmarking. This prioritization helps product teams decide which features matter most for the ICP segment. The quantified impact ($200K–$1.5M per problem per company) lets product weigh roadmap trade-offs: features that unlock faster attrition prediction have higher ROI than features that add reporting cosmetics.

**CS:** After implementation, CS teams can measure whether PeopleGraph is solving these three problems. Did attrition shift? Did headcount forecast accuracy improve? Did the company complete a pay equity review and make adjustments? These are measurable, customer-observable outcomes that feed retention and expansion.

PeopleGraph's strongest fit within the target segment (Series B–C tech, 1,000–5,000 employees, $50M–$500M ARR) clusters around three problems that create $500K–$2M+ in annual impact: unplanned attrition during rapid growth, inaccurate headcount planning that forces reactive hiring decisions, and compensation equity blind spots that drive attrition of valuable talent. Each problem is grounded in specific buyer pain, quantified in terms the economic buyer (CFO) and execution owner (CHRO) care about, and maps to product capabilities that address root cause rather than symptom.
