# Your Alerts Stopped Meaning Something — And You Didn't Notice Until It Cost You

You get 50 alerts a day across your monitoring stack. Datadog fires for a CPU spike. CloudWatch fires for an RDS latency bump. Prometheus fires for a Kubernetes deployment. Most days, it's noise. Some days, it's the real thing buried under 87 simultaneous pages.

That's when your team stops trusting the alerts.

The cost compounds quietly. Your on-call engineer responds to the 15th false positive of the week and treats the 16th page the same way — slowly, skeptically, late. That's when a critical incident that should take 5 minutes to diagnose takes 20. By then, your customer has been down 30 minutes. SLA breach. Escalation. Retention risk.

This isn't a discipline problem. You've tuned the alerts. You've reviewed them. You've added more rules. And every week, more noise floods in — because you're managing rules in three separate tools, and none of them know what the others are doing.

**Here's what shifts:** When your alerts mean something again, so does your on-call rotation. Pages become actionable. Your best SREs stop leaving because the job becomes manageable. Your platform team stops spending 25% of their capacity maintaining alert rules and starts shipping the infrastructure work that should have shipped six months ago.

This is the gap in your current setup. You have the data. You have the tools that generate alerts. You have the tools that coordinate response. What's missing is the thing that sits between them — that looks across your entire stack, figures out which alerts actually correlate to real incidents, and suppresses the noise before it reaches your on-call team.

That's the layer that should exist. And the math is simple: teams that fill this gap reduce noise by 40-60% in the first month, cut on-call pages by two-thirds, and recover platform team capacity they thought was gone forever.

Your alerts can mean something again. But only if someone is looking across all of them at once.
