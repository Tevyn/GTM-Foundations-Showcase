# Annotations: Write a competitive brief on a buyer in ICP evaluating a competitor

---

> [!v1-passage]
> We're in a mid-stage evaluation with a **Fortune 500 financial services company** (~12,000 employees, 40+ branch and office locations across North America). The VP of Corporate Security is leading the evaluation. They currently run **Genetec Security Center** as their VMS with **Software House** access control across all sites. Verkada entered the deal after the prospect attended a Verkada-sponsored security conference panel last quarter.

> [!v2-passage]
> Consolidated Precision Manufacturing (CPM) is a **$2.1B aerospace parts manufacturer** with four facilities across the Midwest, running **620 cameras on Genetec VMS** with Honeywell access control and a 36-person guard force. Their VP of Global Security, **Mark Ellison**, is evaluating both Ambient.ai and Verkada after a perimeter breach at their Toledo facility in Q4 resulted in $180K in raw materials theft and an insurance investigation that took 11 days to close.

**Challenge:** Engagement & Conversion

**V1:** The original ICP is broad, so the brief opens with a generic company profile and no specific trigger event — the seller walks into the deal without a hypothesis about what's driving urgency.

**V2:** The focused ICP identifies specific buying triggers for this segment, so the brief can ground the entire deal narrative in the event that actually opened the conversation.

**Impact:** Sellers who walk into competitive deals without a clear hypothesis about the buyer's urgency lose control of the evaluation to whoever frames the problem first.

> - **Camera-agnostic deployment:** Ambient.ai works with their existing Hikvision and Bosch cameras across all 40+ sites. Verkada requires proprietary cameras, meaning the prospect would need a full hardware swap to get Verkada's AI capabilities.
> - **Access Intelligence (video-PACS correlation):** Ambient.ai correlates video with their Software House access control data to reduce false alarms by **95%**. Verkada does not offer native video-access control correlation.
> - **150+ verified threat signatures:** Ambient.ai's Threat Detection library covers a significantly wider range of security scenarios than Verkada's basic person/motion detection.

> [!v2-passage]
> **CPM has 620 cameras already deployed.** Verkada's model requires replacing every camera with their proprietary hardware. At CPM's scale, that's a **$1.5M-$3M+ capital project** before any intelligence is delivered — plus months of installation downtime across four manufacturing facilities where production can't stop. Ambient.ai layers onto the existing Genetec/Honeywell infrastructure on day one. This isn't a marginal advantage; it's the difference between a 60-day deployment and a 12-18 month infrastructure overhaul.

**Challenge:** Engagement & Conversion, Account Value

**V1:** The original problem-solution work describes pain at headline level, so the competitive framing defaults to a feature checklist the seller reads off rather than an argument the seller makes.

**V2:** The focused problem-solution work maps the specific cost and disruption of each problem, so the competitive framing builds an argument around what the buyer would actually lose by choosing the alternative.

**Impact:** Feature comparisons invite buyers to shop — when competitive framing is anchored in the buyer's specific cost of switching, it reframes the evaluation around a problem only one vendor solves.

# Reveal

## Engagement & Conversion

**ICP:** Most companies define their ICP as an industry list and a company size range — so the seller walks into every deal with the same generic pitch. When ICP work identifies the specific buyer, their triggers, and what's driving urgency, competitive briefs can frame the deal around the problem the buyer is already trying to solve — and that relevance is what earns the next conversation.

**Problem-solution:** Most companies describe their problem at headline level — "false alarm reduction" or "camera-agnostic deployment" — so competitive framing defaults to a feature comparison chart. When problem-solution work maps the specific mechanism and cost of the pain, sellers can build arguments around what the buyer would lose — and that specificity is what moves buyers from evaluation to decision.

**Positioning:** Most companies position against competitors by comparing feature lists — so every competitive conversation invites the buyer to add more vendors to the eval. When positioning explains why the typical approach fails, sellers can dismiss the alternative as solving the wrong problem — and that reframe is what keeps deals from stalling in multi-vendor comparisons.

## Account Value

**Problem-solution:** Most companies describe the cost of their problem in vague terms — so pricing conversations default to competitor benchmarking. When problem-solution work quantifies the specific dollar impact of the pain, sellers can anchor pricing to the cost of the problem, not the cost of the alternative — and that framing is what protects deal size and reduces discounting.
