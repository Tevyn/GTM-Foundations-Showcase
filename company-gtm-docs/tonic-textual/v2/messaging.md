## Voice & Tone

### Voice Characteristics

| Characteristic | Sounds Like | Doesn't Sound Like |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Specific, not generic** | "Models trained on Textual-de-identified data reach 95% of raw-data accuracy" | "Our solution helps you build better models" |
| **Healthcare-grounded** | "Clinical notes need semantic meaning for models to learn patient context" | "Data needs to be preserved for AI to work" |
| **Evidence-based** | "We've seen this pattern in 200+ health systems" | "We're the industry leader in de-identification" |
| **Buyer-first** | "Your LLM roadmap accelerates when you solve the data utility problem" | "Our product has powerful synthesis capabilities" |

### Tone by Context

- **Website / marketing content:** Informed, clinical (speak the buyer's language, not downstream to patients). Use healthcare examples and terminology. Confident in the approach but not overselling. "Here's what we've learned from 200+ implementations" not "We're revolutionary."

- **Sales conversations:** Conversational, diagnostic. Start with questions about their current approach and pain. When they describe their redaction struggles, reflect understanding ("So models are underperforming because the data's too sparse?"). Then move to solution framing — position as problem-solver, not vendor.

- **Customer success:** Outcome-focused, collaborative. Frame value delivery against the three pillars: "Your models hit X% accuracy, freeing Y FTE, with audit trail complete." Partner posture — "Here's what we're seeing in other customers facing similar challenges."

### Buyer Language Patterns

| Buyers Say | We've Been Saying | Use Their Language |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "Redacted data broke our models" | "De-identification impacts data utility" | Use their exact phrasing. It's the recognition moment. |
| "We need to prove we de-identified everything" | "Audit trail demonstrates systematic coverage" | Don't translate — use "de-identified" language, not "privacy-preserved." Compliance buyers use the former; it's more concrete. |
| "Our engineers are buried in rule maintenance" | "Manual de-identification processes are labor-intensive" | Use "buried" and "maintenance" — it's real frustration language. |
| "How do we ship an LLM without exposing PHI?" | "Data privacy and model performance are sometimes in tension" | Their urgency is real. Don't soften it with hedging language. "You can ship without exposing PHI" is stronger than "we enable privacy-aware AI." |
| "Can we achieve model parity on redacted data?" | "Synthetic data enables equivalent model performance" | Use "parity," "achieve," "on redacted data." It's their question. Answer it directly. |

**The vendor test:** If you replaced "Textual" with "Delphix" or "AWS Comprehend," do the core messages still make sense? If yes, the messaging is too generic. Redo it. Our value isn't "de-identification works." Our value is "synthetic data solves the utility problem better than redaction."

## Talk Track Guidance

### "What do you do?"

**The 30-second version:**
"We enable healthcare organizations to build high-performing AI/ML using unstructured clinical data without exposing PHI. Most approaches choose between privacy and utility — they redact data to be safe, but then models underperform. We solve that by replacing PHI with realistic synthetic equivalents that preserve clinical meaning. So models trained on our de-identified data achieve near raw-data performance, compliance is provable, and data science teams can focus on actual AI/ML instead of redaction maintenance."

**Structure:** Problem (redaction breaks models) → What we do differently (synthesis preserves meaning) → Why it matters (model performance + compliance + efficiency).

### "How are you different?"

**When they ask about redaction tools:**
"Redaction removes data entirely — which solves privacy but destroys meaning. We preserve meaning by replacing PHI, not removing it. Same privacy protection, full utility. The difference shows up immediately: test both approaches on your clinical notes and compare model accuracy."

**When they ask about in-house NER:**
"Building an NER model is easier than operating one. We've solved both. What you avoid: 8-12 months of development, ongoing annotation work, model drift, and tying up a senior engineer on maintenance. You get to focus on actual ML."

**When they ask about AWS Comprehend / cloud vendors:**
"Comprehend is a redaction tool that runs in the cloud. So you avoid the headcount, but you hit the same utility wall — models trained on redacted data underperform. We're solving a different problem than 'easier redaction.' We're solving 'how do I get high-performing models without privacy risk.'"

**Positioning principle:** Lead with the buyer's actual criterion (model performance, cost, speed), not the product's feature. The buyer evaluates on outcomes, not capabilities.

### Language to Avoid

- **"We're a platform that..."** — This is vendor-speak. Use: "We enable you to..." or "We help your team..."
- **"Our solution leverages..."** — Leverages what? Be specific. "Our healthcare-specialized NER model" is better than "our advanced AI capabilities."
- **"De-identification solution"** — Vague. Use the specific approach: "synthetic data generation" or "clinical NER with synthesis."
- **"Privacy-preserving AI"** — Too broad; doesn't differentiate. Use: "De-identification that preserves data utility" or "synthetic data that achieves model parity."
- **"Enterprise-grade"** — Meaningless. Use specific proof: "battle-tested on 10M+ clinical notes across 50+ health systems."
- **"Cutting-edge NER technology"** — Oversells and sounds like you don't trust the substance. Use: "Healthcare-specialized NER trained on real clinical data from 200+ institutions."
- **"The market leader in..."** — Never. Buyers don't care; it's an internal claim. Use specific evidence: "Customers report 95%+ model accuracy on de-identified data" or "Average de-identification cost reduction of 60%."

## Metaphors and Analogies

### The Redaction vs. Synthesis Analogy

**Problem redaction faces:** Redaction is like using white-out on a story — it removes the offensive words but destroys the narrative. The reader can't follow the plot.

**Synthesis approach:** Synthesis is like having an editor rewrite the story — same plot and meaning, different specific words. The reader follows the narrative perfectly.

**Why it works:** Everyone understands what white-out does. The metaphor makes the utility problem instantly clear. Use it when explaining to non-technical buyers (CFOs, board members) why redaction isn't the answer.

**When to use:** In marketing narratives and discovery calls when the buyer is still in "why not just redact?" mode. Reinforces the positioning wedge.
