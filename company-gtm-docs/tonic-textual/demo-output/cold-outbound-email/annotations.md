# Annotations: Write a cold outbound email to a buyer in the ICP

---

**Section:** Here's how the email opens

> [!v1-passage]
> I wanted to reach out because we work with a lot of companies in healthcare, financial services, and insurance who are scaling AI and ML initiatives. A common challenge they're facing: the datasets that would most improve model performance are also the most sensitive — and compliance constraints prevent direct use.

> [!v2-passage]
> I noticed [Health System] is hiring for clinical AI roles and building out LLM workflows — documentation, decision support, claims. That's where the highest-value use cases are.

**Challenge:** Top of Funnel Growth

**V1:** The original ICP covers seven industries without prioritization, so the email opens with a generic observation about companies scaling AI that could be sent to anyone in any of them.

**V2:** The focused ICP knows exactly which signals tell you this buyer is about to hit the wall, so the email opens with a specific hiring and roadmap move the prospect recognizes from their own week.

**Difference:** One opens with a broad industry observation across several sectors, the other opens with a specific hiring signal the buyer is living through right now.

**Impact:** Generic outreach gets deleted. An email that opens with a signal the buyer recognizes from their own week gets read.

**Evidence:** Personalized outreach sees up to 18% response rates — 3–10x higher than generic emails.

---

**Section:** How the email describes the problem

> [!v1-passage]
> Are you dealing with this? Teams often have access to high-quality data like clinical notes, customer conversations, or financial records, but manual redaction approaches break the data apart. They end up choosing between data that's safe but unusable, or data that's useful but risky.

> [!v2-passage]
> Here's the pattern across 200+ health systems: when data science teams fine-tune on redacted notes, accuracy drops 10-15%. "Patient is a smoker from rural Ohio whose mother has hypertension" becomes "Patient is a [REDACTED] from [REDACTED] whose mother has [REDACTED]." The model learns nothing.

**Challenge:** Engagement & Conversion

**V1:** The original problem-solution work stays at symptom level across every segment, so the email hedges by asking whether the reader has the problem and then describing it in abstract terms.

**V2:** The focused problem-solution work maps the specific workflow where redaction destroys the data, so the email can state the problem as observed fact and show the buyer exactly what's happening.

**Difference:** One asks a rhetorical question and describes the problem abstractly, the other states the pattern as fact and shows what it looks like in the buyer's own data.

**Impact:** Buyers understand their problems; they need evidence you actually know theirs. A vague description reads as a guess and gets ignored.

**Evidence:** Reports indicate that outcome-specific proof can lift email conversion by 31–104% over generic value claims.

**Section:** How the email frames the alternatives

> [!v1-passage]
> Tonic Textual is a platform that detects PII and PHI in unstructured data — text, audio, documents — and de-identifies it while preserving context. Instead of crude redaction, we replace sensitive information with realistic synthetic alternatives. Our proprietary NER models are tuned for healthcare, finance, and insurance domains. We provide an SDK, UI, and integrations with LangChain and Microsoft Fabric so you can embed privacy directly into your data pipelines.

> [!v2-passage]
> The obvious move is better rules or building your own NER. But 8 months in, you've tied up a senior engineer on maintenance and the model still underperforms — you're treating the symptom, not the root cause.

**Challenge:** Engagement & Conversion

**V1:** The original positioning leads with product capabilities, so the email pivots from the problem into a feature and integration list any vendor could swap their logo onto.

**V2:** The focused positioning explains why the typical in-house approach falls short for this buyer, so the email dismisses the default path as a peer observation instead of listing features.

**Difference:** One describes the product's capabilities and integrations, the other names the path the buyer is already considering and explains why it stalls.

**Impact:** Buyers move faster when they see their problem named precisely and the wrong path called out. A feature list just invites a side-by-side.

**Evidence:** Buying groups that align on the problem — not just the solution — are 2.5x more likely to close (Gartner).

# Reveal

## Top of Funnel Growth

**ICP:** Most companies define their ICP as a list of industries and a company size range, so every asset has to speak to everyone at once. When ICP work identifies the specific buyer and the signals that tell you their pain is acute, outreach can open with a moment the prospect is living through — and that specificity is what earns attention and turns budget into pipeline.

**Problem-solution:** Most companies describe their problem at headline level — "compliance constraints" or "data quality challenges" — so their outreach sounds like every other vendor's. When problem-solution work maps the specific workflow that burns time and money, content can describe the buyer's real situation, and that specificity is what breaks through noise and earns engagement.

**Positioning:** Most companies position against a list of competitors and lead with features, so their outreach invites comparison shopping. When positioning explains why the typical approach fails for a specific buyer, outreach can dismiss the default path as a peer observation — and that clarity is what builds recognition instead of competing on feature lists.

**Messaging:** Most companies have a different value story on their website, in their sales deck, and in their outreach, so the same buyer hears three different products. When messaging is organized around a single idea, every touchpoint reinforces the same promise — and that consistency is what compounds into trust and pipeline.

## Engagement & Conversion

**ICP:** Most companies define their ICP too broadly to assert anything about the buyer with confidence, so sellers hedge and ask instead of telling. When ICP work identifies one buyer type and their priorities, content can reflect the buyer's situation as observed fact — and that directness is what moves buyers from skim to reply.

**Problem-solution:** Most companies stop at symptom-level descriptions of the pain, so content sounds the same as every other vendor's. When problem-solution work maps the specific mechanism — the workflow, the sequence of events, the quantified cost — content can show the buyer their actual situation, and that specificity is what earns the next click.

**Positioning:** Most companies position by listing features and naming competitors, so outreach invites side-by-side shopping. When positioning explains why the typical approach fails, sellers can reframe the buyer's default path as the real problem — and that clarity is what shortens deal cycles and improves conversion.

**Messaging:** Most companies build outreach as a stack of independent feature claims, so nothing connects and nothing sticks. When messaging is organized around a single idea, every sentence reinforces the same story — and that coherence is what keeps buyers engaged through the full message.
