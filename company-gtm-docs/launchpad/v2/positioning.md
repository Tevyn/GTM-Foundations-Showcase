# LaunchPad — Positioning

## Overview
LaunchPad is positioned for enterprise SaaS marketing leaders whose campaign volume has outgrown their team's ability to coordinate it manually — specifically, VP of Marketing and demand gen leaders at $50M-$500M ARR companies running 12+ concurrent campaigns across Marketo, Salesforce, and paid ad platforms. The strongest right to win is in campaign orchestration: the coordination layer that sits above the tools these teams are already locked into. This positioning anchors on the buyer's experience of fragmentation — not a technology pitch about integration — because the decision to add an orchestration layer happens when the cost of coordination failure becomes undeniable and the alternatives (hiring, consolidating, or building DIY automation) each carry visible tradeoffs the buyer has already evaluated.

## Product Vision
Enterprise SaaS marketing teams invest millions in their martech stacks — Marketo for automation, Salesforce for pipeline, LinkedIn and Google for demand — and then spend a quarter of their ops capacity manually coordinating between them. The tools are powerful individually. The coordination between them is manual, fragile, and invisible until something breaks. LaunchPad exists because campaign orchestration at scale is a distinct problem that none of the individual platforms were designed to solve, and the cost of leaving it unsolved — wasted spend, missed launch windows, attribution gaps, and ops burnout — compounds with every campaign added. LaunchPad is the orchestration layer that makes the stack these companies already own work as a coordinated system instead of a collection of disconnected tools.

## Market Frame of Reference

### The Category We're Defining
**Category:** Campaign orchestration platform

**Why this frame:** The buyer's problem is coordination across systems, not automation within a single system. This distinction matters because it determines which alternatives the buyer evaluates — and therefore where LaunchPad's advantage is clearest. If the frame is "marketing automation," buyers compare LaunchPad to Marketo and HubSpot, which are entrenched incumbents with deeper single-system capabilities. If the frame is "workflow automation," buyers compare to Zapier, which is cheaper and more general-purpose. Neither frame serves the buyer or the product.

"Campaign orchestration" activates the right evaluation criteria: Does this coordinate across my existing tools? Does it reduce the manual work between systems? Does it give me visibility into what's happening across campaigns, not just within one? These are the questions LaunchPad answers best. The frame also clarifies what LaunchPad is not — not a replacement for Marketo or Salesforce, not a general automation engine, not an analytics platform. It's the layer above all of them, purpose-built for the coordination problem that emerges when you're running serious campaign volume across a multi-tool stack.

This frame creates a competitive set the buyer recognizes: native platform orchestration features (Salesforce Flow, Marketo Journey Orchestrator), DIY automation (Zapier/Integromat workflows), the hiring option (add ops headcount), and the consolidation option (collapse everything into HubSpot). Each of these is a real alternative buyers evaluate — and each has a specific failure mode at the scale and complexity of this ICP.

### How Buyers Evaluate Alternatives
The evaluation typically starts with a trigger — a coordination failure that cost real money or a mandate to scale campaign volume without proportional headcount growth. The VP of Marketing or demand gen director starts by asking their ops team: "Can we fix this with what we already have?" That question drives the first round of evaluation toward native platform capabilities and DIY automation. Only when those options reveal their limits does the conversation shift to purpose-built orchestration.

Buyers evaluate in a specific order: first, can the existing stack handle it (lowest friction); second, can we build it ourselves (perceived cost control); third, should we simplify the stack (consolidation); and last, should we add a specialized layer (highest perceived risk, highest potential payoff). LaunchPad enters the conversation at that fourth stage — which means the positioning must acknowledge the buyer has already considered and partially rejected the first three options.

**What buyers consider and why:**

**"We should be able to do this with Salesforce Flow and Marketo Journey Orchestrator"**
Buyers try this first because it requires no new vendor, no new budget approval, and no new integration. Native orchestration works well within a single system — Salesforce Flow handles Salesforce logic, Marketo Journey Orchestrator manages email sequences. Where it breaks: the moment coordination needs to span Marketo, Salesforce, LinkedIn, and Google Ads simultaneously. Native tools can't see audience state across platforms, can't validate UTM consistency across channels, and can't enforce budget guardrails that span ad accounts. Buyers who've invested 8-12 weeks building native orchestration workflows typically discover this limitation when they realize Salesforce Flow can't sync to LinkedIn and Marketo simultaneously without custom development. By then, the sunk cost makes the decision harder, not easier.

**"Let's build Zapier workflows to connect everything"**
Buyers consider this because Zapier is familiar, low-cost, and feels like it keeps control in-house. It works for simple, low-volume sync tasks. Where it breaks: campaign orchestration at scale requires more than data movement — it requires validation (are UTMs correct?), coordination logic (is the audience synced before launch?), and monitoring (did the sync actually fire?). Zapier moves data; it doesn't know whether the campaign is ready to launch. Buyers who've gone down this path describe maintaining 6-10 Zapier workflows that break when Marketo schemas change, require engineering time to debug, and provide no visibility into whether campaigns are actually coordinated. The hidden cost — 30-40 hours per quarter maintaining and debugging workflows — typically exceeds the cost of purpose-built orchestration by the second quarter.

**"Maybe we should just consolidate into HubSpot"**
Buyers consider this when stack complexity feels overwhelming and simplification sounds like relief. HubSpot's unified platform eliminates the coordination problem by eliminating the multi-tool stack. Where it breaks: for companies already locked into Salesforce Enterprise and Marketo Enterprise — with years of customization, integrations, and team expertise built around those platforms — consolidation means a 6-12 month migration, retraining, and abandoning proven tools. The VP of Marketing who chose Marketo and Salesforce for good reasons is rarely willing to rip them out. This option appeals most to companies that haven't yet invested deeply in their stack; for this buyer, it's usually rejected once the migration cost and timeline become real.

**"Let's just hire another ops person"**
Buyers default to this because headcount is the traditional solution to capacity problems. Where it breaks: hiring takes 6-12 weeks to full productivity, adds $200K in annual loaded cost, and solves the capacity problem without solving the coordination problem. Two ops people managing spreadsheets are still managing spreadsheets — just with more hands. Buyers recognize this when they realize the second hire will spend the same 25-30% of their time on manual coordination that the first person does. The economics shift when headcount is frozen or when the VP can see that orchestration tooling at half the cost of a hire delivers 3-4x the capacity multiplier.

## Value Wedge
LaunchPad is the only campaign orchestration platform built specifically for the coordination layer between enterprise marketing tools — the above-stack position that Marketo, Salesforce, and ad platforms were never designed to occupy. This isn't a feature advantage; it's a structural one. Every other option buyers evaluate either works within a single system (native orchestration), connects systems without campaign intelligence (Zapier), or eliminates the multi-tool stack entirely (HubSpot consolidation). LaunchPad is the only option that makes the existing stack work as a coordinated system — preserving the enterprise team's investment in Marketo, Salesforce, and paid channels while eliminating the manual coordination tax that grows with every campaign added.

The way a customer would describe it to a peer: "We were spending a quarter of our ops team's time just coordinating between Marketo, Salesforce, and our ad platforms — making sure audiences were synced, UTMs were right, campaigns weren't overlapping. LaunchPad sits on top of all of it and handles the coordination automatically. Our ops person went from managing 8 campaigns while drowning to managing 20 without breaking a sweat. We didn't have to rip out anything or hire anyone."

**Why this wedge holds:**
- **Buyers perceive it as meaningful:** The coordination problem is the one these buyers live with daily. When they hear "orchestration layer above your existing stack," the response is recognition — "that's exactly what's missing" — not confusion. The pain of manual coordination is visceral for ops managers and demand gen directors; the cost of it is visible to VPs and CFOs. The wedge connects to both the operational frustration and the economic consequence.
- **It's ownable:** Native platform orchestration can't occupy this position because Salesforce and Marketo are structurally incentivized to keep customers within their own ecosystems, not to coordinate across competitors' platforms. Zapier can't occupy it because general-purpose workflow automation lacks the marketing-specific intelligence (audience overlap detection, UTM validation, campaign-aware sync timing) that makes orchestration reliable at scale. HubSpot can't occupy it because their value proposition is consolidation, not coordination — they want to replace the stack, not sit above it. LaunchPad's position is structurally defensible because it requires being vendor-neutral across the enterprise martech ecosystem, which no incumbent platform vendor will choose to be.
- **It resonates on contact:** Buyers who've experienced coordination failures — overlapping audiences, broken UTMs, campaigns launched with incomplete sync — hear "above-stack orchestration" and immediately map it to their specific pain. The wedge doesn't require explanation; it requires a nod of recognition. Sales teams report that the "orchestration layer" framing consistently produces the response: "That's exactly what we've been trying to build ourselves."

## Positioning Statement
> For VP of Marketing and demand gen leaders at enterprise SaaS companies who are running 12+ campaigns across Marketo, Salesforce, and paid channels — and losing ops capacity, launch speed, and attribution accuracy to manual coordination between those tools — LaunchPad is the campaign orchestration platform that coordinates the entire stack as one system. Unlike native platform orchestration, DIY automation, or stack consolidation, LaunchPad preserves the enterprise team's existing tool investments while eliminating the coordination tax that scales with campaign volume — because it was built for the above-stack layer those tools were never designed to occupy.

**How to use this statement:**
This is the spine, not the script. Marketing uses it to frame campaign narratives: every campaign should connect back to the "coordination tax" problem and the "above-stack orchestration" solution — not to feature lists. Sales uses it to anchor the opening of a conversation: lead with the buyer's coordination problem, not with LaunchPad's capabilities. The first 30 seconds should make the buyer feel understood, not pitched. Product uses it to evaluate roadmap decisions: features that strengthen the above-stack orchestration position get priority; features that pull LaunchPad into competing with Marketo or Salesforce on single-system capabilities erode the wedge. If a GTM decision doesn't trace back to this statement, question whether it strengthens or dilutes the position.
