# V1 Anti-Patterns — How Most B2B Startup Content Actually Looks

## Purpose

V1 content is a realistic representation of what Series A-B B2B SaaS companies typically produce. It's not obviously terrible — it looks professional, uses correct grammar, has decent structure on the surface. The problem is subtler: it doesn't actually work. It doesn't resonate with buyers, doesn't differentiate from competitors, and doesn't move people through the funnel.

V1 exists so teams can see the gap between "what we have" and "what's possible." When placed side-by-side with V2, the contrast makes the value of strong GTM foundations viscerally obvious.

The key to generating good V1: it must feel real. Teams should look at V1 and think "yeah, that looks like our blog" or "we've definitely sent one-pagers like this." If V1 feels like a strawman or a parody, it loses its teaching power. These are the kinds of outputs that get approved in review because they look polished — the problems only become visible when you ask "but does this actually work?"

---

## V1 context sourcing and voice/tone

**Always use V1 GTM docs when available.** V1 content must be built from the V1 doc set (`v1/icp.md`, `v1/psf.md`, `v1/positioning.md`, `v1/messaging.md`), not the V2 docs. This matters because the V1 ICP is broad and unfocused (e.g., "data-driven organizations" vs. V2's "enterprise healthcare AI/ML teams"), and the V1 positioning is capability-focused rather than market-framed. The content's weaknesses should flow naturally from the doc set's weaknesses — a broad ICP produces content for "everyone," unfocused positioning produces generic claims, and feature-framed messaging produces feature-forward copy.

**V1 voice/tone should match V1 messaging docs.** The V1 messaging doc typically defines voice with generic brand adjectives ("innovative," "trusted," "bold") and no actionable guidance. The resulting content sounds corporate and vendor-like — the tone of a company talking about itself rather than a person who understands the buyer's world. V1 tone is:
- **Corporate and polished** — grammatically correct, professionally formatted, but lacking personality or point of view
- **Vendor-centric** — "we" and "our" centered, describes the company and its capabilities rather than speaking to the buyer's situation
- **Safe and hedged** — avoids strong claims, uses qualifiers ("can help," "may improve," "enables organizations to"), sounds like it was written by committee
- **Jargon-forward** — relies on industry vocabulary and buzzwords as a substitute for specificity

This should be noticeably different from V2's voice, which is confident, direct, human-centered, and buyer-anchored. The voice gap between V1 and V2 is one of the most visible contrasts in a side-by-side comparison.

## The seven failure patterns

When generating V1 content, apply these patterns consistently. They should work together — a single piece of V1 content will typically exhibit 4-6 of these simultaneously, because they're all symptoms of the same root cause: missing or weak GTM foundations.

### 1. Feature-forward framing

V1 content leads with what the product does, not what the buyer's problem is. The product is the subject of every paragraph. The opening sentence typically starts with the company name or product name.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Blog post opens: "[Company] is a [category] platform that helps teams [capability]..."
- One-pager headline: "[Product Name]: [Capability Description]"
- Landing page hero: "The [adjective] [category] platform for [broad audience]"
- Paragraphs structured as: "[Product] does X. It also does Y. Additionally, it offers Z."

**Why real companies do this:** It's the easiest content to write. The product team can describe what the product does. The marketing team can polish that into professional-sounding copy. Nobody needs to do customer research or positioning work — you just describe the thing you built. This is why feature-forward content is the default mode for any startup that hasn't done deep GTM work.

**V2 contrast:** V2 opens with the buyer's situation, names their problem in their language, and introduces the product only after establishing why the reader should care.

### 2. Generic, interchangeable language

V1 content could belong to any company in the category. Swap the product name and logo, and the content still makes sense. There's nothing company-specific in the positioning, proof, or framing.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Claims like "drive efficiency," "accelerate growth," "empower teams," "unlock value"
- Descriptions that are true of every competitor: "our platform integrates with your existing tools"
- No mention of the company's specific approach, methodology, or differentiation
- Benefits described at the category level, not the company level

**Why real companies do this:** Without clear positioning (market frame, value wedge, competitive contrast), there's nothing specific to say. The content defaults to category-level language because that's all the team has. The marketing team knows the product is different — they just can't articulate how in buyer terms.

### 3. No audience signal

V1 content is written for "everyone" — or at minimum, for a category so broad it's meaningless. There's no signal about who specifically should be reading this, what role they're in, or what problem keeps them up at night.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Blog posts about broad topics ("What is [Category]?", "Why [Trend] Matters") with no audience targeting
- One-pagers that describe the product without naming who uses it or why
- Landing pages with heroes like "The platform for modern teams" — which modern teams? Doing what?
- Content that addresses "organizations" or "businesses" instead of specific roles or company types

**Why real companies do this:** Without a focused ICP, the team is afraid to narrow. "If we say it's for healthcare data science teams, what about our finserv customers?" So they write for everyone, which means they write for no one. The content tries to appeal broadly and ends up resonating with nobody.

### 4. Buzzword-heavy, jargon-dense copy

V1 content substitutes buzzwords and industry jargon for concrete outcomes. It sounds professional but communicates nothing specific. A reader finishes a paragraph and can't explain what the product actually does or what result they'd get.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Heavy use of: leverage, synergy, cutting-edge, next-generation, robust, seamless, holistic, end-to-end, state-of-the-art, innovative, transformative, best-in-class, mission-critical
- Compound jargon: "AI-powered intelligent automation platform"
- Outcome claims that are actually just bigger buzzwords: "drive digital transformation," "enable operational excellence"
- Sentences that require a second read to parse: "Our platform leverages advanced machine learning to deliver actionable insights that empower data-driven decision-making across the enterprise"

**Why real companies do this:** Buzzwords feel safe. They sound professional. They fill space without requiring the team to commit to specific claims that could be challenged. When a startup doesn't have quantified proof points or clear differentiation, jargon fills the gap.

### 5. Feature-based competitive positioning

When V1 content does address competition, it does so through feature comparison — not through the buyer's problem or decision framework. The framing centers the competitor ("Other tools do X, but we have Y") rather than centering the buyer's evaluation criteria.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- "Unlike [Competitor], we offer [feature]"
- Comparison tables based on feature checkboxes, not buyer outcomes
- Positioning framed as "us vs. them" instead of "here's the problem, here's why our approach is different"
- Competitive mentions that make the competitor the subject of the sentence rather than the buyer

**Why real companies do this:** Feature comparison is the default competitive instinct. The product team knows what features competitors have and don't have. Without a value wedge or market frame, the only competitive story is "we have more features" or "our features are better." This makes competitors the main character of the positioning instead of the buyer.

### 6. Misaligned CTAs — always "book a demo"

V1 content defaults to hard-sell CTAs regardless of the funnel stage. Every blog post ends with "book a demo" or "talk to sales." Every one-pager says "contact us." There's no consideration of where the reader is in their journey or what action makes sense given the content they just consumed.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- TOFU educational blog post: ends with "Ready to see [Product] in action? Book a demo today."
- Awareness-stage landing page: hero CTA is "Schedule a Call" or "Request a Demo"
- One-pager for a broad audience: CTA is "Contact Sales" with no intermediate step
- No differentiation in CTA across content types or funnel stages
- Sometimes the CTA is completely disconnected from the content — bolted on as an afterthought in the final paragraph

**Why real companies do this:** The marketing team is measured on MQLs and demo requests. Every piece of content becomes a conversion vehicle for the same action. There's no content strategy that maps different assets to different funnel stages with stage-appropriate next steps. The assumption is: if someone is reading our content, they must be ready to buy.

### 7. Dense, essay-style structure

V1 content reads like a document, not like web content. Long paragraphs, minimal formatting, label-style headers ("Background," "Overview," "Conclusion"), and no scan optimization. The content assumes the reader will start at the top and read every word — which almost no one does.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Paragraphs of 5-8 sentences with no visual breaks
- H2s that are labels ("Overview," "Features," "Benefits," "Conclusion") rather than benefit statements
- No bullets, pull quotes, callouts, or visual hierarchy within sections
- Blog posts structured like college essays: introduction → body → conclusion
- Content that requires reading every word to get the point — no skimming path
- No single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis — everything is dense blocks

**Why real companies do this:** Most B2B content is written by people trained to write documents (reports, briefs, proposals) — not web content. The essay format feels natural because it's how we learned to write in school. Without explicit scan-optimization guidance, content defaults to "wall of text with headers."

## How to apply these patterns by content type

### V1 Blog Post
- Open with the company name or a broad category definition ("What is [X]?")
- Use label H2s: "Background," "The Problem," "Our Solution," "Conclusion"
- Write in 4-6 sentence paragraphs with no bullets or visual breaks
- Include one product mention in the conclusion, feeling disconnected from the body
- End with "Book a demo" or "Contact us to learn more" regardless of topic
- Tone: professional but flat — reads like a textbook entry, not a person with a point of view
- No customer quotes, no specific data, no named examples

### V1 One-Pager
- Headline is the product name + category descriptor: "[Product]: The [Category] Platform"
- Opens with company description: "[Company] is a leading provider of..."
- Lists 4-6 features with one-line descriptions, no outcome framing
- Proof bar (if any) uses vague metrics: "Significant improvement" or unattributed percentages
- CTA: "Contact us" or "Request a demo"
- Dense paragraphs in the body — poor scannability
- Could describe any competitor in the space

### V1 Landing Page
- Hero: "[Product Name] — [Adjective] [Category] for [Broad Audience]"
- Subhead describes the product, not the buyer's outcome
- CTA: "Book a Demo" or "Get Started" (same as every other SaaS landing page)
- Feature section organized by product architecture, not buyer priorities
- Proof section: logo bar with no context, or vague testimonial ("Great product!" — Marketing Manager)
- No problem section — goes straight from hero to features
- FAQ used as a dumping ground for copy that didn't fit elsewhere
- Dense text blocks in each section with minimal visual hierarchy

---

## The feel of V1

V1 should read like content produced by a competent marketing team that hasn't done deep GTM strategy work. The writing is grammatically correct, professionally formatted, and uses appropriate industry vocabulary. It's the kind of content that passes a quick review ("looks good, ship it") but fails when tested against: Does the reader know who this is for? Can they articulate what makes this company different? Do they know what to do next — and does that action make sense given where they are in their journey?

V1 is not sloppy. It's professional-looking content that doesn't actually work. That's what makes it a powerful contrast to V2.
