# Discovery Call Scripts Reference

These 10 scripts follow the arc of a typical discovery call. They build on each other sequentially — each one earns the right to use the next.

## Script 1: Opening the Call

**The phrase:** "I'm glad we found the time to meet today."

**Why it matters:** Most salespeople thank the prospect for their time. This subtly positions you as lower status — as if you're less important than them. Humans are status-oriented. If you're perceived as lower status than your buyer, you carry zero influence.

"I'm glad we found the time to meet today" positions you as equal status. Powerful people say things like that. It's kind, not arrogant — but it doesn't give away your power.

**Second benefit:** It reads the room. After saying this, pause. Their response tells you whether to linger on small talk or jump to business:
- "Yeah, me too, eager to see what you have" → Get to the agenda
- "For sure, how's your day going?" → A few minutes of rapport building is appropriate

## Script 2: Setting Rules of Engagement

**Three keys to the first five minutes:**
1. Align on the **objective** — "The objective of this meeting in my mind is simply to determine if we should have a next step."
2. Align on the **agenda** — "Here's the agenda I'm thinking that will help us get there..."
3. Align on the **decision** — "By the end, I'd like to put you in a position where you can decide ___."

**How to deliver it (softening language is critical):**
- "Here's what I'm thinking in terms of an agenda. Let me know if you had something else in mind..."
- This gives the buyer a sense of autonomy and control, while you're actually making the suggestion
- Close with: **"Does that feel fair?"** — people want to be perceived as fair, so they almost always agree

**Full example (selling pclub.io to VP Sales):**
"Mind if we discuss the agenda? Great. Here's what I have in mind. Let me know if you're thinking something else:

First, the objective of this meeting in my mind is simply to determine if we should have a next step. Obviously I don't expect us to do business on this call. So let's just learn enough about each other to determine whether the next logical step even makes sense. Fair?

Great. Now here's the agenda I'm thinking: First, let's spend most of our time getting clear on the skill gaps you're seeing in your sales organization. Once we're clear on that, I can explain what and how we do what we do so we can jointly decide whether we schedule a demo.

Does that agenda feel fair? Great... [transition to discovery]."

**Keep it under 30 seconds.** Don't overcomplicate it.

## Script 3: The Discovery Prompter (Earn the Right)

**When to use:** When the buyer is cold or skeptical. If they're already warmed up and answering questions freely, skip this.

**The technique:** Tell a short (2-5 minute) narrative about a customer's pain — one that mirrors what this buyer likely experiences. The goal is for the buyer to "see themselves" in the story.

**Narrative structure:**
1. "One of the customers we worked with was dealing with [pain statement]."
2. "Before working with us, they were struggling with [expand on pain]."
3. "In fact, it was such a struggle that [negative ramifications]."
4. "The short story is we helped them [achieve desired future state]."
5. **"But enough about our customers... tell me about the key challenges YOU are facing when it comes to [your area]?"**

**Why it works:** "When you can articulate your buyer's pain better than they can, they will automatically assume you have the right solution for them." The trust is immediate. They open up and share their real challenges.

**Example:**
"Before we get into your situation, let me tell you a quick story about the challenges we typically solve, so you have context for the rest of the conversation. Does that feel right? Great...

One of the customers we worked with in your space was dealing with low close rates. Before working with us, their close rates in enterprise had dipped to 19%. They found their salespeople didn't have the skills to sell through a down economy. They were struggling with uncovering and quantifying pain, multi-threading, accessing power, and feature dumping in demos.

In fact, it was such a struggle that their CAC had soared. LTV:CAC dipped from 3:1 to 2:1. Their CFO pulled the plug on new AE hiring until they got efficiency back in order.

The short story is we helped close almost all of those skill gaps. While their close rates aren't yet back in the mid 20s, we've managed to get them to 22% and we're trending in the right direction.

Anyway. Enough about our customers and their situations... help me understand YOURS. What challenges are showing up in your business?"

## Script 4: Uncovering the Need Behind the Need

**The key question:** "What's going on in your business that's driving this challenge to be a priority?"

**Why this matters:** Most salespeople stop at the first answer. The buyer says "we need visibility into sales conversations" — and the seller thinks they've found the problem. But "visibility" is a *solution*, not a problem. You need to keep digging.

**The progression (from the Gong example):**
- Surface: "We need visibility into sales conversations" (solution, not problem)
- Deeper: "Our reps are struggling to access power — stuck with mid-level managers" (getting warmer)
- Deeper still: "It's showing up in the length of our sales cycle — 9 months vs. target of 6" (the real business impact)
- Deepest: "It's affecting cash flow. 11 month delay from sales cycle start to cash. We may need to raise dilutive capital." (the strategic imperative)

**The pattern:** When buyers share a surface-level answer, ask what's *driving* that to be a priority. The further down you go, the bigger the problem — and bigger problems lead to bigger deals.

## Script 5: Diagnosing Root Cause

**The question:** "What's your opinion on why this is happening?"

**Why pain alone isn't enough:** A buyer can tell you they have a long sales cycle (pain). But *why* it's happening determines what solution they'll buy. If the cause is "we're selling too low and not reaching the CIO," then showing features about "coaching reps on next steps" won't land — because next steps weren't the root cause.

**The cause of the problem dictates the solution that buyers purchase.** You need to align your solution's capabilities to the root cause, not just the symptom.

People love giving their opinion. If they trust you (and they will if the earlier scripts worked), they'll give long, rich, valuable answers. Their answers let you align your demo and proposal to what *actually* needs fixing.

## Script 6: Quantifying Pain

**The question:** "What metric is suffering as a result of the challenges you're sharing with me?"

**Positive variant:** "What metric would improve the most if you solved the challenges you've been sharing with me?"

**Why this matters:** This starts the business case conversation. It gets the buyer to connect their pain to a number — close rates, sales cycle length, revenue per rep, etc. Once a metric is on the table, you can quantify the financial impact of the problem (and therefore the value of solving it).

This won't flesh out the entire business case on its own, but it starts the quantification process — often the hardest part.

## Script 7: Summarizing (Transition + Trust)

**The structure:**
"Let me summarize what I've heard so far...
[Summary using many of the buyer's own words]
Did I get that right?"

**Critical detail: Use the buyer's exact words, not your paraphrase.** Buyers resonate with *their* words. When you parrot their exact language back — especially emotional words — they perk up. They lean in. They emotionally exhale.

**Benefits of summarizing:**
1. **Builds trust** — the buyer feels genuinely understood
2. **Buys time** — prevents the interrogation feeling. Summarize every 3-5 questions, then use it as a springboard: "Ok, great. Thanks for confirming that. Now that we have your challenge established, what are the ripple effects this issue is having across the business?"

**Example:**
"Let me summarize what I've heard so far... You're looking to upskill your account executive team. They are struggling with a number of critical skills, including: building champions early in the deal, multithreading instead of getting stuck with one contact, uncovering and quantifying business pain in a way that generates urgency, and accessing power to build productive relationships with senior execs.

When you look at how this is showing up in the business, it comes down to close rates. They're sitting at 18% where you used to enjoy a 23% close rate. While much of that can be attributed to the down economy, it's also clear that your reps could use help with skills that will help them close deals despite an economic downturn...

Did I get that right?"

## Script 8: Building Negative Impact

**Three techniques to build negative impact without feeling salesy:**

**Tip 1 — Give a reason for the question:**
"Hey I have a somewhat obvious question for you. The reason I'm asking this is, if we get far enough down the road where we decide we want to do business together, your CFO is probably going to want an answer to this: **What are some of the negative ramifications you're seeing [problem they shared] have on the business?**"

**Tip 2 — Use sophisticated language:**
Don't ask "how does this impact you?" — that sounds like a salesperson.
Ask: **"What are the ripple effects this challenge is having across the business?"**
Same question, but it signals business acumen and competence.

**Tip 3 — Connect to named stakeholders:**
"How is this showing up for [specific person/team they've mentioned]?"

**Five benefits of building negative impact:**
1. Build urgency (greater negative impact → greater bias for action)
2. Increase deal size (greater impact → larger willingness to pay)
3. Increase access to power (greater impact → more executive attention)
4. Justify the cost (greater impact → sturdier business case)
5. Service your customers (helps them appreciate the full magnitude of the problem)

## Script 9: Understanding Buying Criteria

**The question:** "What do you think you need in a solution to solve these challenges?"

**Why this matters:** Even if you've uncovered deal-closing pain, diagnosed root cause, quantified impact, and built urgency — you can still lose if the buyer's mental model of the solution doesn't match what you sell.

Their answer tells you where to start: they might describe exactly what you offer, or they might be thinking about a completely different category of solution. Either way, you need to know so you can align your product or reshape their thinking.

## Script 10: Securing Next Steps (What, Who, Why)

**The pattern:** Top performers always have a clear POV on three things:
- **What** should the next step be?
- **Who** should be involved (buyer's side and seller's side)?
- **Why** are you recommending this, and why should those people be there?

**The script:**
"Looks like we're coming up on time. Should we talk about next steps?

Great. You know your company better than me. So if you have a different idea than this, let me know.

But based on what you told me today, what I recommend we do next is ___.

It would be helpful if we could include [names] in that meeting too to get their perspective.

Does that feel fair?"

**Why this works:** Selling is an act of leadership. The seller has 10x more experience than the buyer in how these purchases unfold. They should have a point of view — strong, but loosely held. Not "so what do you think we should do next?" but a clear recommendation the buyer can accept or modify.
