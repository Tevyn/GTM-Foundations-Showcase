# One-Pager Reference — Marketing Collateral

## Purpose

A one-pager is a single-page marketing asset used at events, in campaigns, on the website download center, or as a leave-behind. It needs to work on its own — without a presenter, without context, without a sales rep to explain it. That constraint shapes everything: the content must be self-contained, instantly scannable, and persuasive enough to earn a second look.

Unlike a sales enablement one-pager (which supports a conversation), a marketing collateral one-pager starts the conversation. It's often the first substantive thing a buyer reads after a headline or ad. The standard it has to clear: within 10 seconds, the reader knows what this is, who it's for, and why they should care.

---

## Structure

Every one-pager follows a problem → solution → outcome arc with proof woven throughout. The specific structure adapts based on whether the content is persona-specific or capability-focused.

### Default structure (capability or use-case focused)

```
1. HEADLINE — Outcome-oriented, states the promise (not the product name)
2. SUBHEAD — Narrows the promise, signals who this is for
3. PROBLEM FRAME — 2-3 sentences. The buyer's current reality and what's broken.
   Use a hook stat if available (e.g., "Only 3% of buyers fill out forms...")
4. SOLUTION BRIDGE — 2-3 sentences. How the product changes the situation.
   Connect the problem to the specific capability. Not a feature list.
5. PROOF BAR — 3-4 metrics with customer attribution.
   Format: [Big number] + [What it measures] + [Customer logo or name]
   Example: "+250% increase in outbound pipeline — Bynder"
6. VALUE PILLARS — 3 pillars, each with:
   - Benefit-led heading (not a feature name)
   - 2-3 bullet points: what it does, in terms of what the buyer gets
   - Keep each pillar to ~40 words
7. CTA — Funnel-appropriate next step. One CTA, clear and specific.
8. COMPANY BOILERPLATE — 1-2 sentences. What the company does, who it serves.
```

### Persona-specific structure

When the one-pager is written for a specific buyer role (e.g., "For Revenue Leaders" or "For Data Science Teams"):

```
1. HEADLINE — Speaks to this persona's priority, not a generic outcome
2. SUBHEAD — Names the role or function and their core challenge
3. WHAT YOU CARE ABOUT — 2-3 sentences framing the problem through this
   persona's lens. What's on their plate, what pressure they're under,
   what they've already tried.
4. HOW WE HELP — 2-3 sentences mapping the product to their specific needs.
   Not the full product — just what's relevant to this role.
5. PROOF — A mini customer story or 2-3 metrics relevant to this persona's
   evaluation criteria (e.g., a CIO cares about TCO; a data science lead
   cares about model accuracy).
6. WHAT'S NEXT — Persona-appropriate CTA. A technical buyer might want a
   product tour; an executive might want an ROI estimate.
7. COMPANY LINE — 1 sentence, tailored to what this persona values.
```

## Quality checklist

Before finalizing a one-pager, verify:

- [ ] The headline is outcome-oriented, not product-descriptive. Test: does it describe a result the buyer wants, or a thing the product is?
- [ ] A reader can understand the core message in under 10 seconds by scanning just the headline, subhead, and pillar headings
- [ ] The problem frame uses buyer language, not product language. It describes a situation the reader recognizes.
- [ ] Proof points are specific (numbers, customer names/types, timeframes) — not vague ("leading organizations," "significant improvement")
- [ ] Each value pillar heading communicates a benefit, not a feature name. "Cut de-identification time by 70%" not "Automated De-identification."
- [ ] The CTA matches the funnel stage. A top-of-funnel one-pager should not say "Book a demo."
- [ ] The content is genuinely single-page. In markdown, this means roughly 300-450 words of body copy. One-pagers that require scrolling defeat the purpose.
- [ ] A competitor could NOT put their logo on this. There's something company-specific in the positioning, proof, or framing.

## What to avoid

These are patterns from real B2B one-pagers that weaken the content:

**The feature catalog (page 2 syndrome):** The one-pager starts strong with a problem/solution framing, then devolves into a list of product features with one-line descriptions. This happens when the second half of the one-pager is treated as a mini product tour instead of continuing the persuasion arc. Every element on the page should support the argument, not inventory the product.

**The wall of text:** Dense paragraphs with no visual hierarchy. One-pagers live or die on scannability. If the reader can't extract the key message from headings and proof points alone, the content fails.

**Generic proof points:** "+30% improvement" without attribution, context, or specificity. A metric without a customer name or company type is noise. If real metrics aren't available, describe the type of outcome and mark it as a placeholder.

**The "About Us" opening:** Starting with "Company X is a leading provider of..." immediately signals vendor-speak. The reader doesn't care who you are until they know you understand their problem.
