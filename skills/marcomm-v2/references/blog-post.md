# Blog Post Reference

## Purpose

A blog post is long-form content (800-1500 words) published on the company blog. Unlike a one-pager or landing page, a blog post has room to develop an idea, build credibility through depth, and earn organic traffic. But that room is also a trap — it's easy to produce 1200 words of generic content that says nothing specific.

The skill asks the user what type of blog post they want, because the structure, tone, and success criteria are different for each. If the user doesn't specify, infer from the topic and ask to confirm.

---

## Blog post types

### Type 1: Thought Leadership

**Purpose:** Stake a claim, challenge conventional thinking, position the company (or a named author) as a credible voice in the space. Less product, more perspective.

**When to use:** The topic is a trend, a shift in how things are done, a contrarian take, or a "here's what we've learned" piece. The company wants to be known for a point of view, not just a product.

**Structure:**
```
1. H1 — Provocative or specific enough to earn the click. Should preview the claim.
   Bad: "The Future of B2B Marketing"
   Good: "Why Your ICP Document Is Probably Hurting You"

2. OPENING (2-3 paragraphs) — Set up the tension. What does the conventional
   wisdom say? What's wrong with it? What have you seen that contradicts it?
   The reader should feel "this person gets it" within the first 100 words.

3. THE CLAIM (1 paragraph) — State the thesis clearly. This is the "so here's
   what we actually think" moment. One clear, defensible claim.

4. THE ARGUMENT (3-5 sections with H2s) — Each section supports the thesis
   with evidence, examples, or reasoning. H2s should read as a mini-outline
   of the argument — a reader who only scans H2s should get the gist.

   Each section: lead with the point (first 1-2 sentences), then support it.
   Use specific examples — named companies, real scenarios, concrete data.
   Avoid hypotheticals when you can use actuals.

5. IMPLICATIONS / SO WHAT (1-2 paragraphs) — What should the reader do
   differently based on this perspective? Make it actionable.

6. CTA — Soft, funnel-appropriate. A thought leadership post is typically
   TOFU/awareness. CTA into a related guide, framework, or deeper resource.
   Never "book a demo" on a thought leadership post.
```

**Tone:** Confident, opinionated, specific. The author has a point of view and earns the right to share it through evidence and experience. Not preachy. Not hedged.

### Type 2: Educational / How-To

**Purpose:** Teach the reader something useful. Establish authority through helpfulness. May reference the product but isn't product-led — the value is in the knowledge, not the pitch.

**When to use:** The topic is a process, framework, methodology, or "how to do X." The company wants to be the place people go to learn about this domain.

**Structure:**
```
1. H1 — Clear, specific, promises a deliverable. Should signal what the reader
   will know or be able to do after reading.
   Bad: "A Guide to Competitive Intelligence"
   Good: "How to Build a Deal-First Competitive Intelligence Program (Step by Step)"

2. OPENING (1-2 paragraphs) — Why this matters now. What's the cost of not
   knowing this? Ground it in a real scenario or common mistake.
   Don't waste time on preamble — get to the value fast.

3. THE FRAMEWORK / STEPS (3-7 sections with H2s) — The core content.
   Each section is one step, principle, or component.

   H2s should be benefit-oriented or action-oriented:
   "Step 2: Map competitive insights to deal stages" not "Step 2: Mapping"

   Each section:
   - Lead with what to do and why (2-3 sentences)
   - Then explain how, with specifics
   - Include an example, template snippet, or "here's what this looks like"
   - Keep sections roughly equal in depth

4. COMMON MISTAKES / PITFALLS (optional but strong) — What most people get
   wrong. This signals expertise — you've seen enough implementations to know
   where they break.

5. SUMMARY / KEY TAKEAWAYS — Bullet the 3-5 most important points.
   These should stand alone for skimmers who jump to the bottom.

6. CTA — TOFU or MOFU depending on the depth. Educational content can push
   slightly harder than thought leadership — a checklist, a template download,
   a webinar that goes deeper. Still not "book a demo."
```

**Tone:** Helpful, authoritative, practical. The author is a teacher, not a salesperson. Use "you" and "your." Give the reader credit — don't over-explain basics.

### Type 3: Product-Led

**Purpose:** Show how the product solves a specific problem or enables a specific workflow. More product-forward than the other types, but still anchored in the buyer's problem — never a feature tour disguised as a blog post.

**When to use:** A new feature launch, a use case deep dive, a "how customers use X to do Y" piece. The product is the subject, but the buyer's problem is the frame.

**Structure:**
```
1. H1 — Frames the problem or outcome, not the feature.
   Bad: "Introducing Our New AI-Powered Objection Handler"
   Good: "How to Turn Competitive Objections Into Win Themes (Using AI)"

2. OPENING (1-2 paragraphs) — The problem this product capability solves.
   Start with the buyer's situation, not the product announcement.
   "Your reps hear the same five objections every week..." not
   "We're excited to announce..."

3. THE APPROACH (2-3 paragraphs) — How this product solves the problem.
   This is where the product enters. Frame it as the solution to the
   problem you just described, not as a feature list.

4. HOW IT WORKS (3-5 sections with H2s) — Walk through the workflow or
   capability in concrete terms. Each section shows a step or aspect of
   the product in action.

   Use specific scenarios: "When a rep logs a competitive objection about
   pricing, Klue automatically..." not "The platform enables users to..."

   Include screenshots or describe what the user sees if visuals are
   available.

5. RESULTS / PROOF — Customer example or quantified outcome. What changes
   for the buyer when they use this? A before/after comparison is strong here.

6. CTA — MOFU or BOFU depending on the feature maturity. New feature →
   product tour or interactive demo. Established capability → case study
   or trial signup.
```

**Tone:** Still problem-first and buyer-anchored, but can be more direct about the product. The product is the protagonist of the story, but the buyer's problem is the plot.

## Universal blog rules

These apply to all three types:

**Strong H1s get clicks.** The title is the single highest-leverage element. It should be specific enough to promise value, provocative enough to earn the click, and honest enough to deliver. Test: would YOU click on this if you saw it in a feed? Strong patterns for thought leadership titles: "From X to Y" (positions a shift), "Why [specific audience] Must [specific action]," or a specific contrarian claim. Weak patterns: "The Future of [Broad Topic]," "A Guide to [Category]," anything that could title 50 different articles.

**Intros earn the read.** You have 2-3 sentences to convince a reader to stay. Don't waste them on throat-clearing ("In today's fast-paced business environment..."). Start with tension, a specific claim, a surprising stat, or a vivid scenario. Build momentum through short, staccato paragraphs — each one tightening the tension until you land the thesis.

**Short paragraphs, always.** B2B blog readers skim. Keep paragraphs to 1-3 sentences. Use single-sentence paragraphs deliberately for emphasis — they create pacing and punch. A wall of text signals "this isn't worth reading." A page of short, punchy paragraphs signals "this person respects my time." This is not a suggestion — it's a core structural principle.

**H2s are a mini-outline.** A reader who scans only the H2s should understand the shape of the argument. Each H2 communicates a benefit, action, or question — never a label.

**Front-load every section.** The first 1-2 sentences of each section carry the key point. Readers who skim horizontally (F-pattern) will only read these. Don't bury the lead in paragraph three.

**Proof earns trust.** Every claim should be supported by: a specific data point, a named example, a customer quote, or a concrete scenario. Unsupported claims read as marketing fluff. Customer quotes should include full attribution (name, title, company) and be woven throughout the post — not saved for a single testimonial block. If you don't have proof, describe what type of proof would go here and mark it `[PROOF POINT]`.

**Avoid the feature slide deck.** Product-led blog posts are not feature tours. Every feature mentioned must be in service of a problem the buyer has. If you remove the problem context and it reads like release notes, rewrite.

**Word count guidance:** 800-1500 words. Thought leadership can run longer if the argument is dense. Educational posts should be as long as the framework requires and no longer. Product-led posts tend shorter (800-1000) because the product does some of the talking.
